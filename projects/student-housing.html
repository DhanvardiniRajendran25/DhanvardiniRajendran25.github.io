Student Housing Platform

A full product cycle to fix Boston's broken student-housing search, grounded in market research and 100+ stakeholder interviews.

My role
Product Manager: research, scoping, feature definition, and the business case
Context
Northeastern Product Club · a 1,500-member student community
Focus
End-to-end product cycle, from discovery to a measurable outcome

The problem

Finding student housing in Boston is one of the most stressful parts of arriving at a new university, and it never seemed to get easier. Listings live in a dozen disconnected places, trust is hard to establish, and students burn weeks toggling between tabs with almost no signal about what is actually a good fit. I wanted to treat this not as a one-off frustration but as a real product problem worth a full cycle: understand it deeply, then ship against it.

Who it is for

The work was anchored in a concrete community: the 1,500 members of the Northeastern Product Club, most of them students moving into the city and facing the same housing scramble at the same time of year. That gave me a real, reachable audience to research with and design for, rather than an abstract market. Their journeys, from first search to signed lease to move-in, became the spine of the whole project.

100+students surveyed

Discovery

I surveyed more than 100 students to map how they actually search, where they get stuck, and what makes them give up on a lead. Synthesizing those responses surfaced 10 recurring pain points spread across the whole journey: scattered discovery, low trust in listings, repetitive paperwork, unclear pricing, and a clunky path to move-in among them. The common thread was clear: students spend an enormous amount of time searching with very little signal, and no single tool serves the end-to-end journey.

The approach

PRINCIPLE

Fix the journey, not just the listings. Most tools optimize one slice of the search. I scoped a platform that follows the student from discovery all the way to move-in, so the friction does not just move somewhere else.

Prioritize by pain

I mapped the 10 pain points against how often they came up and how much time they cost, then used that to decide what made the first build and what waited.

Reduce time-to-signal

Every feature was judged on one question: does it help a student reach a confident decision faster? That kept the scope honest and the feature set tight.

Ship a focused set

I defined and shipped 7 features that together covered the highest-cost moments in the journey, rather than a long list that addressed everything shallowly.

Key decisions

The hardest calls were about what to leave out. Several pain points were real but low-frequency, so I deliberately deferred them to keep the first release sharp and usable. I also chose to design around the existing behavior students already had, meeting them where they searched rather than asking them to adopt an entirely new habit. The bet was that a smaller set of features, aimed squarely at the costliest moments, would move the outcome more than broad but shallow coverage.

The outcome

The shipped feature set cut the average housing search time by 75% for the students it served. That number mattered because it tied directly back to the pain I started with: the journey was slow and full of dead ends, and the work measurably shortened it. Putting a real metric on the result also made the case to others that this was a product worth continuing to invest in, not just a nice idea.

What I would measure next

Search time was the right first metric, but I would extend the picture over time: how many searches end in a signed lease, how trust in listings changes, and whether students who use the platform report less stress through the move-in window. I would also watch which of the 7 features carry the most weight, so the next cycle invests where the impact actually concentrates rather than spreading evenly. The takeaway for me was that a full product cycle, grounded in real research and a single honest metric, beats a longer feature list every time.
