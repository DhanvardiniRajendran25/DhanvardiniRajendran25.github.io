Lyft Redesign

A rider-app redesign built on a pyramid of needs and three principles: nail the basics, build confidence through consistency and transparency, then earn brand-distinct delight. I mapped six core problems and went beyond the obvious fixes.

My role
Product Manager
Context
Self-directed UX case study
Focus
6 problems, 7 fixes, 4 deeper proposals
View the wireframes

The problem

A rider opens Lyft in moments that are rarely calm: late at night, in an unfamiliar city, juggling bags one-handed at the curb. In those moments the app has to answer a few questions instantly. Is this my car? What will this cost? Where is the driver actually facing? Auditing the rider experience, I found the app added doubt at exactly these high-stakes points, so I scoped a redesign around restoring confidence rather than adding features.

The framing: a pyramid of needs

I structured the redesign as a pyramid of needs resting on three principles, so that reliability got fixed before polish was ever reached for. The order is deliberate: a delightful animation means nothing if the rider still cannot tell which car is theirs.

1. Nail the basics

Make every core choice clear and obvious, so the primary task never requires a second guess, even one-handed on a small screen.

2. Build confidence

Earn trust through consistency and transparency: the same patterns everywhere, and no hidden cost or surprise along the way.

3. Earn delight

Layer brand-distinct delight only on top of a foundation that already works, never as a substitute for it.

Six problems I mapped

Working through the rider flow, I isolated six friction points, each one quietly working against trust at a different stage of the journey.

Unclear driver and vehicle ID

At pickup, the rider could not quickly confirm the driver, car, and plate, the single most safety-critical match in the whole flow.

No upfront fare transparency

The price was not surfaced clearly before committing, leaving riders to brace for a surprise at the end of the trip.

Non-directional car icons

Map icons did not show which way the car was facing, making it hard to read whether the driver was actually approaching.

Poor one-handed ergonomics

Key actions sat outside comfortable thumb range, awkward in exactly the on-the-move contexts where the app is used.

A vague "Request Lyft" CTA

The main call to action did not make the consequence of the tap clear enough at the decisive moment.

Buried contextual actions

Time-sensitive actions like Cancel and Send ETA were hidden, just when a rider most needs them within reach.

The official fixes

Against those problems I aligned seven direct fixes, each one removing a doubt rather than adding a feature, and each mapping back to a principle: clarity for the basics, transparency for confidence.

Upfront fare estimates

Show the price before the request, so cost stops being a surprise waiting at the destination.

Enhanced driver card

Surface photo, car, and plate together so the rider can confirm the match at a glance.

Directional car icons

Orient map icons to heading, so a rider can read approach direction instantly.

Bottom-tab consolidation

Pull primary actions into thumb range to fix one-handed reach.

Contextual pre-arrival actions

Promote Cancel and Send ETA to the moment they are actually needed.

Scheduling, Lyft Pink, and safety

Round out the experience with scheduled rides, Lyft Pink, and expanded safety tooling.

My deeper proposals

Beyond the obvious fixes, I pushed four proposals that go further than the official redesign, each one targeting a failure mode that only shows up under stress: a dead connection, a hidden safety tool, an invisible support path, or a confusing pricing feature.

Resilient offline fallback

When the app freezes, a "weak connection, retrying" banner plus a Lite Mode keeps the core flow usable instead of leaving the rider stranded on a blank screen.

Persistent safety strip

For safety features that stay hidden, contextual prompts, a persistent safety strip, and onboarding toggles bring PIN and Share Ride into view.

Tiered support button

A persistent, tiered support button (Emergency 911, Support, FAQs) that pulses in high-risk scenarios, so help is never buried.

Price Lock volatility indicator

For Price Lock confusion, a route-health volatility indicator, a pre-window price preview, scope tags, and lock-impact alerts make the feature legible.

Designing for trust

DESIGN PRINCIPLE

Confidence is the feature. In a product people use while stressed and in motion, every screen either adds doubt or removes it, so I treated reliability and transparency as the real deliverables and delight as the layer on top.

What I took away

Five lessons held across the work. Reliability equals trust. Safety must be visible, not hidden. Transparency beats surprise pricing. Proactive, contextual prompts drive adoption better than buried settings. And accessibility, from Lite Mode to contrast, is not optional. The through-line I carried out of the project: in a product people reach for under pressure, you build confidence by nailing the basics first, and only then earn the right to delight.
