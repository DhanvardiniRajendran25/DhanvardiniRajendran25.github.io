SpaceX Hardware Development

SpaceX treated rockets like software, shipping, failing, and iterating in public while traditional aerospace optimized for never failing at all. The interesting part is when each culture is actually right.

My role
Product Manager
Context
Iterative hardware development, failing forward
The bet
Build, fly, fail, learn, repeat
Principle
Match process to cost of failure

The situation

SpaceX develops vehicles like Starship by building, flying, and intentionally pushing prototypes to failure, gathering real flight data fast rather than perfecting designs on paper first. Legacy aerospace primes do the opposite, optimizing for exhaustive analysis and a successful first flight because, in their world, a failure is a catastrophe. Two cultures look at the same engineering problem and reach opposite conclusions about how to learn, and the interesting part is that each can be right depending on the context.

Two philosophies of learning

Fail forward

Ship a prototype, fly it, expect it to break, and treat each failure as paid-for data. Speed of iteration is the asset.

Analyze first

Model and review everything so the first flight succeeds. Certainty is the asset because failure is unaffordable.

The real question

Not which culture is better in the abstract, but which one fits the cost and reversibility of failure in a given domain.

The core question

When does a fast, fail-forward iteration loop beat traditional aerospace's analyze-everything rigor, and where would copying it be reckless? I wanted to resist the easy narrative that SpaceX is simply braver or smarter, and instead find the conditions under which each approach is the correct one.

Analysis: iteration speed vs cost of failure

This is fundamentally an agile versus waterfall bet applied to hardware. SpaceX shortens the build-test-learn loop so each failure buys real data cheaply, and that only works because vertical integration and in-house manufacturing make a new prototype fast and relatively cheap to produce. The whole model hinges on driving the cost and cycle time of a single iteration low enough that learning by failing becomes rational rather than wasteful. Change that one variable and the entire strategy flips.

Tradeoffs: where fail-forward holds and breaks

THE SCOPING RULE

Fail-forward is powerful only where failure is affordable. It works when failures are uncrewed, cheap to replace, and produce information you cannot get from analysis. It is irresponsible the moment a failure becomes catastrophic, irreversible, or able to kill someone.

This is why the same company that blows up Starship prototypes runs rigorous, conservative processes for crewed Dragon flights. The iteration culture is not applied everywhere; it is deliberately scoped to the domains where failure is cheap, and switched off where it is not. The discipline is in knowing which mode you are in.

My takeaway

The transferable principle is not "move fast and blow things up," it is match your process to your cost of failure and your speed of iteration. As a PM I would push hard for cheap, fast loops in low-stakes domains where a mistake is a learning, and deliberately switch to high-rigor, low-iteration discipline the moment failures become irreversible or unsafe. The skill is not picking a culture; it is reading the stakes correctly and choosing the loop that fits them.
