02 · Executive summary

A trusted answer, not just an answer.

Employees misread dense policy documents, lean on informal verbal guidance, and create real regulatory exposure. The two existing options are both bad: route every question to legal (slow and expensive), or use a generic chatbot that confidently hallucinates policy (dangerous). SAGE sits in between, grounded in the actual policy text, automated, auditable, and secure.

The product bet is that a hardened RAG agent with structured output, built on GPT-4o and orchestrated with LangGraph, can answer a question like “I saved customer data to my personal laptop, is that a violation?” with a citation-backed ruling a non-lawyer can act on, while refusing to be talked out of the rules. Every query passes a multi-layer prompt-security pipeline before it reaches the model, and every answer returns Answer, Citations, Risk Level, Reasoning, and Confidence.

03 · Background & context

Why a grounded compliance agent, and why now.

Compliance knowledge lives in long, formal policy documents that most employees never read end to end. The cost of getting an answer wrong is asymmetric: a single mishandled-data incident or a misread eligibility rule can create regulatory exposure that dwarfs the cost of the question. Generic LLM assistants make this worse, not better, because a confident hallucination of a policy detail is more dangerous than no answer at all.

SAGE was built as the full lifecycle of an LLM application across five phases: system-prompt design, retrieval and hardening, an agent architecture, production features, and adversarial security. Accuracy climbed at every phase, from a 52% zero-shot baseline to 91%+ on hard cases, and the security work proved the system against a structured attack suite. It is deployed on Hugging Face Spaces, where a reviewer can load one of five demo organizations or upload their own policies and ask.

Context

SAGE is portable by design: drop in any company’s PDF or text policies and a dynamic system prompt specializes reasoning per organization type. The requirement is that the product never depends on a single hard-coded corpus, so the same agent serves any policy set without code changes.

04 · Problem statement & evidence

Policy is dense, and getting it wrong is expensive.

Problem: employees cannot reliably self-serve compliance answers from raw policy text, so they either escalate every question to a slow, expensive legal team or guess, and a generic chatbot that confidently hallucinates policy detail is not a safe substitute.

  • Long policy documents are read selectively or not at all, so real rules are missed.
  • Informal, verbal guidance spreads incorrect interpretations that nobody can audit later.
  • A generic assistant invents plausible policy text, which is worse than abstaining because it reads as authoritative.
  • A single scenario often triggers several policies at once, and conflicts between them go unnoticed until an incident.

Who feels it most: the compliance reviewer drowning in repeat questions, the policy owner whose documents are misread, and the employee who just wants a safe, sourced answer they can act on without waiting on legal.

Evidence from the build

A zero-shot generic model answered the evaluation set at only 52% accuracy, and adversarial cases (false-fact injections and jailbreaks) were a distinct, large slice of the evaluation corpus precisely because confident wrong answers are the core failure mode. SAGE’s whole value is closing that gap with grounding, citations, and refusal.

05 · Target users, personas & JTBD

Three users, one trusted answer.

The Compliance Reviewer

Fields the same policy questions over and over and is the escalation point when an answer is unclear. Needs an auditable, citation-backed trail for every ruling.JTBD: “When an employee asks a policy question, help me resolve it correctly and defensibly without re-reading the whole manual.”

The Policy Owner

Owns and maintains the policy documents and cares that they are interpreted as written, and that conflicts between policies surface early.JTBD: “When my policies are queried, make sure answers are grounded in the real text and tell me where two policies collide.”

The Employee

Has a real situation and a yes-or-no question, and wants a safe answer in plain English without flagging themselves to legal for routine queries.JTBD: “When I’m unsure if something is allowed, give me the actual rule, the risk level, and where it came from, so I can act.”

Representative user stories

  • Employee, eligibility: “I started 45 days ago, am I eligible for remote work?” goes to the actual rule, the section it came from, a risk level, and a confidence score.
  • Employee, data handling: “I saved customer data to my personal laptop, is that a violation?” goes to a grounded ruling with the triggered policies and a High/Medium/Low risk level.
  • Policy owner, conflict: “Do our remote-work and data-security policies contradict each other here?” goes to a surfaced tension before the final answer is written.
  • Reviewer, audit: “Show me the trail for that answer” goes to a logged query, retrieved sections, risk, and citations.

06 · Goals, success metrics & non-goals

What success looks like.

North Star metric · TRR

Trusted Resolution Rate (TRR), the share of compliance questions SAGE resolves with a correct, citation-grounded answer the employee can act on without escalating to legal. It only counts if the answer is right, sourced to real policy text, and safe.

Goal tiers

  • User goal: get a correct, sourced, plain-English ruling with a risk level fast, without escalating to legal.
  • Business goal: deflect routine compliance questions from legal while reducing the rate of unsafe or unaudited guidance.
  • Model goal: answer only from retrieved policy text, cite every claim, abstain when there is no evidence, and resist every prompt-injection attempt.

Guardrail rule

A citation is mandatory on every claim, and the system abstains rather than guess. The hallucination, citation-failure, and injection-block guardrails are release gates: a build that regresses any of them does not ship, regardless of how high its accuracy score climbs.

Non-goals (v1)

  • SAGE does not give legal advice or replace a lawyer; it surfaces grounded policy and a risk level for a human to act on.
  • It does not edit, draft, or version the company’s policy documents.
  • It does not invent policy where none exists, and it does not answer out-of-scope (non-policy) questions.
  • It does not make a binding decision; the human stays in control of the action.

10 · AI capability & system behavior

What the system does, and what it must never do.

A LangGraph ReAct agent on GPT-4o reasons over a ChromaDB hybrid-RAG index of section-chunked policy text. It calls tools to retrieve, cross-reference, detect conflicts, and assess risk, then returns structured output. Free-form generation never happens; only grounded, in-scope queries reach the model.

Inputs and outputs

DirectionContract

OutputAnswer, Citations (real policy sections), Risk Level (High/Medium/Low), Reasoning (with any tension), Confidence (score).

What it must never do

Never invent a policy or a section number. Never assert a rule from outside the retrieved text. Never be talked out of the rules by an instruction in the query. Never answer an out-of-scope question as if it were policy. Never emit an answer without citations and a risk level when policy support exists.

11 · Data & retrieval requirements

The grounding contract.

SAGE’s answers are only as trustworthy as its retrieval. The policy corpus is section-chunked so that every citation maps to a real, addressable unit of policy text, and retrieval is hybrid (semantic plus keyword) and re-ranked so that both conceptual and exact-term matches surface.

RequirementSpecification
CorpusAny company’s PDF or text policies; 5 demo organization types supported out of the box.
ChunkingSection-level chunks so citations are addressable at policy-section granularity.
RetrievalHybrid: semantic plus keyword, re-ranked (0.6 semantic / 0.4 keyword), with query expansion across compliance synonyms.
Citation granularityCite to the specific section, not the whole document; every cited section must exist in the corpus.
Recall@kThe triggered policies for a scenario should appear in the top-k retrieved chunks; measured against the evaluation set’s expected sections.
Precision@kRetrieved chunks should be on-topic for the scenario, so the agent reasons over signal, not noise.
PermissionsRetrieval is scoped to the active organization’s corpus; no cross-org leakage of policy text.
EfficiencyRetrieve and inject only relevant chunks; the hybrid pipeline cut prompt tokens by roughly 80% versus injecting the full corpus.

12 · Model & prompt strategy

GPT-4o, orchestrated and pinned.

The reasoning model is GPT-4o, chosen for structured-output reliability and instruction-following under adversarial pressure. LangGraph orchestrates a ReAct agent so the model reasons with tools rather than free-generating, and a dynamic system prompt specializes reasoning per organization type while pinning identity and the rules in every call.

Prompt strategy

  • Technique selection: 13 prompting techniques were compared head to head (zero-shot, chain-of-thought, ensembling, self-consistency, self-criticism, meta-prompting, and more); the winners were synthesized into the production prompt.
  • Output contract: the prompt forces structured output, Answer / Citations / Risk / Reasoning / Confidence, so a generative model becomes a sourced one.
  • Identity lock: the system prompt is re-asserted in every call with authority-claim resistance and org-mismatch detection baked in, so an injected instruction cannot override the role.
  • Grounding instruction: answer using only the retrieved excerpts; abstain rather than reach outside them.

Prompt versioning

Prompts are treated as product surface area, not throwaway strings. Each phase produced a tracked prompt version, scored against the evaluation set so a change that helped one case but hurt overall accuracy was caught. The defense pattern set was versioned the same way, expanding from 10 to 52 patterns across phases.

How the framework is built

LayerWhat it does
Golden eval datasetA curated set of scenario cases, each labelled with the expected risk level, the policies it should trigger, and the relevant sections, including a large slice of no-evidence and adversarial cases.
Human rubricA scored rubric (groundedness, citation correctness, risk accuracy, hallucination, answer quality, abstention, calibration) applied by a human reviewer, shown as the table above.
Automated evaluatorsDeterministic checks for risk-label match, citation-resolves-to-corpus, and groundedness that run on every build without a human in the loop.
LLM-as-a-judgeAn independent model scores answer quality (accuracy, clarity, completeness) at ≥8.5 / 10, used as a fast proxy on top of the deterministic checks, never as the sole gate.
Baseline + thresholdsThe 52% zero-shot baseline anchors progress; each metric has a release threshold (for example ≥91% risk accuracy, 100% injection block, near-zero hallucination).
Regression testingEvery prompt or defense version is re-scored against the full golden set so a change that helps one case but hurts overall accuracy is caught before it ships.
Adversarial / red-team testingThe structured attack suite plus live red-team rounds across 9 attack families run as a standing part of evaluation, not a one-off.
Segment-level evalAccuracy is read per organization type and per case type (single-policy, multi-policy, conflict, no-evidence, adversarial) so a strong average never hides a weak segment.

Cadence + ownershipThe PM (Dhanvardini) owns the eval set and thresholds; the suite runs on every prompt or defense change and on a fixed cadence, with red-team rounds repeated as new injection techniques emerge.

LLM-as-a-judge, and its limits

The LLM judge is a useful, cheap proxy for answer quality, but it is not ground truth. It can be biased toward longer or more confident-sounding answers, it can miss a subtle factual or citation error, it is not fully reproducible run to run, and it can in principle be swayed by content in the answer it is scoring. So the judge never decides correctness alone: risk-label match, citation-resolves-to-corpus, and groundedness are scored by deterministic evaluators and human review, and the judge sits on top as a quality signal, calibrated against human labels and never used as the release gate by itself.

15 · Safety, privacy & security

Auditable, scoped, and honest.

  • Grounding as safety: the primary safety control is that SAGE answers only from retrieved policy text and abstains otherwise, so a confident hallucination cannot become an authoritative-looking ruling.
  • Auditability: every answer logs the query, retrieved sections, risk, and citations, so any ruling can be defended or reviewed after the fact.
  • Scoped data: retrieval is limited to the active organization’s corpus; one company’s policy text never leaks into another’s answers.
  • Human in control: SAGE surfaces grounded policy and a risk level; the human makes the decision. It does not give binding legal advice.
  • Honest uncertainty: low-confidence answers are labelled and conflicts are shown, rather than papered over.

16 · Prompt-injection & adversarial security

Built to be attacked, then proven against it.

Because the whole product is trust, SAGE was hardened against every known prompt-injection technique: direct injection (persona overrides, instruction smuggling), indirect injection (false attribution, hypothetical framing), prompt exfiltration, and social-engineering authority pretexts. The defense expanded from 10 to 52 patterns across 9 attack families, plus authority-claim resistance and org-mismatch detection baked into the prompts. Against the structured attack suite, the result was a 100% block rate with no legitimate query rejected.

17 · Analytics & experimentation

Instrument the bet.

  • TRR instrumentation: track self-serve resolution and legal-escalation rate against live employee questions to measure the North Star in production.
  • Counter-metrics: monitor hallucinated-citation rate, attack-bypass rate, false-positive blocks, and vague-fallback rate so quality gains are not bought with safety.
  • Prompt experiments: the head-to-head technique comparison is the experimentation harness; new prompt or defense versions are scored against the evaluation set before they ship.
  • Feedback to fine-tune: a planned loop turns low-confidence answers into new evaluation cases, so the eval set grows with the hard questions the system gets wrong.

Model & prompt change management

Prompts and defense patterns are versioned production artifacts, not throwaway strings. A model or prompt change follows the same path every time: propose, re-score against the full eval and attack suites, check it against the guardrail release gates, then promote with the new version pinned into the per-response trace.
