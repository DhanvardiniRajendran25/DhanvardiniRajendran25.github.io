H&M

An end-to-end mobile shopping-app prototype for H&M, designed with full happy-path and error-state coverage across four flows: Shopping & Checkout, Authentication, Order Tracking, and a Chatbot. The strength is the thoroughness of the states.

The problem

Fashion e-commerce asks shoppers to judge fit, style, and value from images and copy alone, and most prototypes only ever show the happy path. I set out to design a complete H&M shopping app where the flows hold up not just when everything works, but when payments fail, emails already exist, passwords break the rules, and invoices do not load. The interesting design work, I decided, lives in those moments most demos quietly skip.

The scope at a glance

Rather than a single linear demo, I built a connected map of screens spanning four flows, each carrying both its success path and the states where something goes wrong.

The four flows

I mapped four flows end to end, designing each not as a single path but as a branching structure that accounts for where real shoppers get stuck.

Shopping & Checkout

Home to Category to Product Detail to Cart to Checkout and Place Order to Order Success, with a Payment Error branch when the transaction does not go through.

Authentication

Become a Member signup, Sign In, and Forgot Password, including an email-already-exists case, signup and signin error states, and password rules of 8 to 25 characters.

Order Tracking

Account to Orders to Order Details to Order Tracking, with an invoice error state for when the document fails to load.

Chatbot

Opens to suggestions and moves on to suggestion results, giving shoppers a guided path when they are unsure what to search.

Designing for the unhappy path

My approach was to treat every screen as one half of a pair: the success state and the state where something goes wrong. The member signup funnel carries its validation errors, the email-already-exists case gets a real screen, the password field both enforces and explains the 8 to 25 character rule, checkout has a Payment Error branch, and order tracking accounts for an invoice that fails to load. Each unhappy state is designed to tell the shopper exactly what happened and what to do next.

Why thoroughness was the point

DESIGN STANCE

A flow is only as trustworthy as its worst moment. Most prototypes look polished because they only ever render success; this one earns trust by showing how the experience behaves when a payment fails, an email is already taken, or an invoice will not load.

The artifact

The result reads less like a strategy paper and more like an interaction-design and flow-architecture artifact. Its value is in the completeness: a connected map of screens and states, from Order Success to Payment Error, from a clean signup to a blocked one, that shows how the whole experience behaves rather than just how it looks at its best. It is the kind of map an engineering team could build against without discovering gaps mid-sprint.

What I took away

Designing the error states taught me that thoroughness is a feature. A flow is only as trustworthy as its worst moment, so I would measure recovery: whether shoppers who hit a Payment Error, an existing-email block, or a failed invoice still complete their task. Coverage of the unhappy path, not just the happy one, is what separates a demo from a shippable experience.
