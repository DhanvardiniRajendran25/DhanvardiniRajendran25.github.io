02 · Executive summary

A sourced answer, not three hours of airtime.

Over 5 million podcasts hold thousands of hours of expert knowledge, yet audio is fundamentally unsearchable: you cannot Ctrl+F a two-hour conversation. PodcastIQ indexes 300+ episodes across 25 channels and layers a nine-agent system on top, so a user can search by concept, compare how experts disagree, watch a claim evolve across years, and fact-check assertions against the live web, each answer cited to the exact timestamp.

The product bet is that combining vector search with graph traversal (GraphRAG), plus a temporal knowledge graph and hybrid fact-checking, can answer questions a transcript search engine cannot, in under five seconds, with a source on every claim. A Router classifies each query into one of eight intents and dispatches a dedicated specialist; grounded synthesis returns an answer the user can verify in one click.

03 · Background & context

Why a podcast-intelligence agent, and why now.

Podcast knowledge is locked inside audio files with no queryable index. Finding every expert opinion on a topic across 100 episodes means listening to all 100; tracking how an expert’s view shifted between 2022 and 2025 is impossible by hand; and claims made on air go unchecked. A generic transcript search engine helps a little but cannot reason over relationships, time, or truth.

PodcastIQ was built across an 11-week project as the full lifecycle of an LLM application: a Snowflake warehouse ingests and chunks transcripts, Cortex embeds and indexes them, a claim-extraction pass populates a Neo4j knowledge graph, a temporal layer classifies opinion drift, and a LangGraph router fans queries out to eight specialist agents, all behind a guardrail stack and an independent GPT-4o judge. It is deployed through a Streamlit interface with a chat surface, a graph explorer, and a channel dashboard.

Context

PodcastIQ is not a “search podcasts” tool. It introduces four technically novel components: GraphRAG (graph plus vector retrieval), a temporal knowledge graph that classifies opinion drift, hybrid fact-checking that minimizes paid web calls, and two-tier speaker attribution that identifies who said what without audio diarization.

04 · Problem statement & evidence

Audio is unsearchable, and claims are unaccountable.

Problem: spoken-word knowledge is trapped in audio files with no queryable index, so users either listen to whole episodes to find one answer or give up, and there is no way to compare speakers, track how a view changed, or check whether a claim holds up.

  • Finding every expert opinion on a topic across 100 episodes means listening to all 100.
  • There is no way to track how an expert’s opinion changed between 2022 and 2025.
  • Claims made on podcasts cannot be automatically fact-checked against the open web.
  • Cross-podcast analysis, comparing what different hosts say about the same topic, is impossible at scale.

Who feels it most: engineers who need a precise, timestamped answer, researchers who track how thinking moves and whether bold claims hold up, and students who want the gist of an episode plus a next thing to watch, all of whom need a sourced answer, not three hours of airtime.

Evidence from the build

The first extraction sorted by view count, which clustered episodes in late 2025 and left the temporal layer too thin to observe drift. A date-spread query diagnosed it, and a time-stratified re-extraction added 36 episodes across 2022 to 2024 in minutes, giving the corpus the multi-year span needed to actually detect 243 claim-evolution pairs (144 contradicted). The need for a temporal layer was not assumed; it was a measured gap in the data.

06 · Goals, success metrics & non-goals

What success looks like.

North Star metric · TTTA

Time-to-Trusted-Answer (TTTA), the median time for a user to go from a question to a correct, cited answer they can act on without re-listening to the episode. It is the one number that captures the whole bet: retrieval that is fast, generation that is faithful, and a verifiable source on every claim.

Goal tiers

  • User goal: get a correct, sourced answer in seconds, with a one-click link to verify it, instead of listening to whole episodes.
  • Business goal: prove that GraphRAG plus a temporal layer plus fact-checking answers questions a transcript search engine cannot, while staying under the Snowflake credit budget and free API tiers.
  • Model goal: route each query to the right specialist, answer only from retrieved sources, cite every claim to a timestamp, and label uncertainty rather than hiding it.

Abstention & confidence

When retrieval returns no supporting excerpts, the system says it cannot find evidence rather than answering from outside knowledge, and a fact-check that the web cannot settle returns UNVERIFIED rather than a fabricated verdict. Inferred speaker attributions carry an explicit HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW / UNKNOWN label, so the product surfaces uncertainty instead of hiding it.

Non-goals (v1)

  • Audio diarization or re-transcription with Whisper; attribution is inferred, not heard.
  • User accounts, multi-language support, and real-time ingestion.
  • A browser extension or collaborative filtering.
  • Answering medical, legal, financial, or private-information questions; those are out of scope by design.

Speaker attribution (cross-cutting requirement)

Every claim is attributed without audio diarization via a two-tier method: Tier 1 per-channel title-regex (76.9% guest coverage) with an llama3.1-8b fallback, and Tier 2 per-claim LLM inference during extraction (zero extra API cost) with explicit confidence (HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW / UNKNOWN). HIGH maps to MADE_CLAIM, MEDIUM to LIKELY_MADE_CLAIM, and UNKNOWN to DISCUSSED_IN.

10 · AI capability & system behavior

What the system does, and what it must never do.

A LangGraph StateGraph orchestrates one Router and eight specialist agents over a Snowflake Cortex backbone (llama3.1-8b for routing, 70b for synthesis), Cortex Search for hybrid retrieval, and a Neo4j read projection for relationship reasoning. Each specialist owns its own data path and prompt; only grounded, in-scope queries that clear the guardrail stack reach an agent.

03

70b + Neo4j

70b + Brave

Inputs and outputs

DirectionContract
InputA plain-English question, classified into one of 8 intents after passing the guardrail stack.
OutputA synthesized answer with deep-linked sources; for fact-check, a verdict (VERIFIED / FALSE / OUTDATED / DISPUTED / UNVERIFIED) with evidence URLs; for temporal, a narrated drift with both clips.

What it must never do

Never assert a claim in a synthesized answer from outside the retrieved excerpts. Never emit a citation that does not deep-link to a real source. Never present an inferred speaker attribution as certain when confidence is low. Never answer a medical, legal, financial, or private-information question as if it were in scope. Never spend a paid web call on a claim the pre-filter already resolves.

11 · Data & retrieval requirements

The grounding contract.

PodcastIQ’s answers are only as trustworthy as its retrieval. A 15-step pipeline moves raw transcripts into a queryable brain over a six-schema Snowflake warehouse (RAW to STAGING to CURATED to SEMANTIC to APP) plus a Neo4j read projection. Transcripts are chunked into ~120-second windows so every citation maps to an addressable, deep-linkable moment, and retrieval is hybrid (vector plus BM25) and re-ranked so both conceptual and exact-term matches surface.

12 · Model & prompt strategy

Two model tiers, orchestrated and pinned.

The reasoning models are Snowflake Cortex Llama: llama3.1-8b for cheap, fast classification (routing, pre-filter, guardrail) and llama3.1-70b for synthesis (summarize, compare, temporal, graph, insight). LangGraph orchestrates them as a StateGraph so each agent reasons on a purpose-built prompt with an explicit output contract, rather than free-generating. An independent GPT-4o judge grades faithfulness so no model grades its own homework.

Prompt strategy

  • Model tiering: the Router runs on 8b because routing is classification, not reasoning, faster and cheaper, validated by the router eval; synthesis runs on 70b where fidelity matters.
  • Grounding instruction: the Summarize prompt forces “answer using ONLY the transcript excerpts” plus a mandatory inline citation format, which turns a generative model into a sourced one.
  • Structured output: claim extraction returns a strict JSON schema (claim text, speaker, role, attribution confidence, claim type, topic, sentiment) so 8,660 claims load without post-parsing, and attribution happens in the same call as extraction at zero extra cost.
  • Sanitized routing: the Router output is normalized (.strip().upper()) and any unrecognized value falls back to SEARCH so the system never dead-ends.
  • Independent judging: the GPT-4o judge runs at temperature 0 with a JSON response format, only on SEARCH and SUMMARIZE paths that have source chunks to compare against.

Prompts as a cost lever

The fact-check prompt is a cost control as much as a quality one. A 70b pre-filter answers from training knowledge first and resolves roughly 30 to 40% of claims before any paid Brave call, so the free tier (2,000 per month) is never exhausted and total external spend stays in the single-digit dollars.

Automated scores are spot-checked by hand against a fixed rubric so a high BERTScore cannot stand in for an answer that is wrong, ungrounded, or unsafe. Each dimension is rated on a sampled set of answers.

Automated evaluators

  • Routing: exact-match accuracy of predicted intent vs. the labelled specialist over the golden routing set.
  • Retrieval: Precision@1/3/8 and MRR over relevance-judged chunks, plus an LLM relevance judge for borderline cases.
  • Generation: ROUGE-1/2/L for lexical overlap and BERTScore F1 for semantic similarity to the source.
  • Latency and cost: per-agent mean and p95 timing, and a token-budget times Cortex-pricing cost estimate per query.
  • Corpus health: 7 pass/fail domain-KPI threshold checks run directly on live warehouse data.

LLM-as-a-judge: GPT-4o faithfulness

An independent GPT-4o judge runs at temperature 0 with a JSON response format on the SEARCH and SUMMARIZE paths, scoring each answer for faithfulness to its retrieved chunks so no Llama model grades its own homework. It is used as an automated faithfulness evaluator at scale, not as a source of truth.

Limitations of the judge (and how it is bounded)

An LLM judge carries known biases: positional bias (favoring whichever answer comes first), verbosity bias (rewarding longer answers), and self-preference (a model rating outputs that look like its own more highly). It is a faithfulness signal, not ground truth, so its verdicts are spot-checked against the human rubric on a sampled set, it is held to a different model family than the generators it grades, and it is treated as a fast screen that flags answers for human review rather than a substitute for the golden references.

The baseline is a transcript keyword-search engine: it cannot route by intent, reason over relationships or time, or cite to a timestamp, so every routed, sourced answer is the bar PodcastIQ has to clear. The thresholds below are the ship gates each dimension is held to.

15 · Safety, privacy & security

Screened, scoped, and honest.

Every user message passes validate_query() before any agent runs: a 5-layer stack ordered cheapest-to-most-expensive, so the costly LLM check fires only when the free checks pass.

LayerCheckCost
1Query length (3 to 500 chars)0ms
2Prompt-injection (12 regex patterns)~1ms
3Language (Unicode-range; over 20% non-English rejected)~1ms
4Scope (medical / legal / financial / private-info)~1ms
5LLM semantic SAFE / UNSAFE (llama3.1-8b)300 to 600ms
  • Grounding as safety: synthesized answers come only from retrieved excerpts, and an independent GPT-4o judge checks faithfulness on the RAG paths, so a confident hallucination cannot pass as sourced.
  • Fail-open by design: if Cortex is unavailable, Layer 5 skips, so infrastructure hiccups never block valid questions; the cheap deterministic layers still run.
  • Scoped data: the corpus is public podcast transcripts; no user accounts or private data are stored, and out-of-scope private-info questions are rejected at Layer 4.
  • Honest uncertainty: every answer appends a standing disclaimer that attributions and verdicts are AI-generated and should be verified against the linked source.
  • Human in control: PodcastIQ surfaces sourced answers and confidence; the user reads the receipt and decides, rather than the system asserting truth.

16 · Prompt-injection & adversarial security

Screened before any model call.

Because the product is trust, an adversarial query must never reach an agent or burn a paid web call. Layer 2 of the guardrail stack screens 12 prompt-injection patterns deterministically before the semantic check, and the Router’s output is sanitized and bounded to the 8 known intents so a malformed or injected classification cannot redirect the system. Untrusted content (transcript text, web results) is treated as data, not instructions: synthesis answers from excerpts and the fact-check verdict is constrained to a fixed label set, so injected text inside a source cannot rewrite the agent’s behavior.

17 · Analytics & experimentation

Instrument the bet.

  • TTTA instrumentation: track router accuracy, p95 latency, and citation deep-link usage against live queries to measure the North Star in production.
  • Counter-metrics: monitor the GPT-4o judge UNVERIFIED rate, the blocked-query rate, and Brave web-search spend so speed and breadth are not bought with faithfulness or cost.
  • Eval as harness: the six-part suite is the experimentation harness; a new prompt, model tier, or retrieval change is scored against router, retrieval, generation, latency, cost, and domain-KPI dimensions before it ships.
  • Feedback to corpus: a planned loop turns low-confidence answers and contradicted claims into new evaluation cases, so the eval set grows with the hard questions the system gets wrong.

19 · Edge cases & fallbacks

What happens when it’s not clean.

Edge caseBehavior
Ambiguous intentThe Router falls back to SEARCH so the query still returns sourced chunks rather than dead-ending.
No relevant chunks retrievedSynthesis reports that it cannot find supporting excerpts rather than answering from outside knowledge.
Cypher generation failsThe Knowledge Graph agent retries up to 3 times with a self-healing loop before reporting it cannot resolve the relationship.
Fact-check claim already knownThe Stage 2 pre-filter resolves it from training knowledge, with no paid web call.
Brave rate limit or outageThe pre-filter absorbs ~30 to 40% of claims; uncertain claims return UNVERIFIED rather than a fabricated verdict.
Cortex unavailableLayer 5 of the guardrail skips (fail-open) so valid questions are not blocked by an infrastructure hiccup.
Low-confidence attributionThe claim is tagged DISCUSSED_IN with explicit low confidence rather than asserting a speaker.
Out-of-scope or unsafe queryRejected at the scope or semantic layer with a clear message, before any agent runs.

20 · Dependencies, risks & assumptions

What this depends on, and what could go wrong.

Dependencies

  • Snowflake Cortex for LLMs (llama3.1-8b / 70b), embeddings (Arctic Embed M), and Cortex Search.
  • LangGraph for the 9-agent orchestration; Neo4j (local Docker) for the graph read projection.
  • Brave Search API for live fact-checking; the YouTube API and yt-dlp for transcript extraction.
  • OpenAI GPT-4o as the independent faithfulness judge; Streamlit for the interface.

Risks & mitigations

Assumptions

  • The corpus is representative enough that drift and comparison answers are meaningful.
  • A user will open the deep-linked source to verify a high-stakes answer rather than trusting it blindly.

Observability & per-response traceability

Every response is traceable to the exact configuration that produced it, so a regression or a bad answer can be reproduced and root-caused rather than guessed at. Each answer carries a trace of the pieces below.

Traced fieldWhat it pins down
Model versionThe exact Cortex model per step (llama3.1-8b routing, 70b synthesis) and the GPT-4o judge build.
Prompt versionThe versioned prompt used for routing, synthesis, extraction, and judging, so a prompt change is attributable.
Retrieval configTop-k, hybrid vector plus BM25 settings, re-rank, and the chunk IDs actually injected.
Route & guardrail traceThe classified intent, the guardrail layers cleared, and any fail-open path taken.
Judge verdictThe GPT-4o faithfulness score and label attached to the answer for sampling and audit.

Model & prompt change management

  • Prompts and the evaluation suite are version-controlled product surface area, not loose strings, so every change has a diff and an owner.
  • No model-tier or prompt change ships without re-running the full golden suite; a negative delta on any dimension blocks it.
  • Model versions are pinned per agent so an upstream model swap is a deliberate, evaluated decision rather than a silent drift.
  • The per-response trace ties a production regression back to the specific model and prompt version that caused it, so rollbacks are targeted.
