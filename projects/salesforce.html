Product Metrics & Strategy

Salesforce CRM Metrics

A product-management critique of CRM dashboards: do activity metrics like calls logged and emails sent actually measure value, or just visible effort? A diagnosis, plus a redesigned measurement framework.

My role
Product Manager
Context
Solo analysis · activity-versus-value critique (Northeastern, INFO 7374)
Focus
Metrics design · leading vs lagging indicators

The question

Easy-to-measure is not the same as valuable. Salesforce dashboards surface calls logged, emails sent, and opportunities updated, but enterprise value lives in closed deals, revenue, and durable relationships that take months to appear. So which is the CRM really measuring? I treated that as a metrics-design problem: the dashboard is the product surface that quietly decides what a sales organization optimizes for, and I wanted to know whether it was pointing at outcomes or just at effort.

The signals in question

Calls loggedactivity, not outcome
Emails sentactivity, not outcome
Opportunities updatedactivity, not outcome
Revenue & retentionthe value that lags

Diagnosis: activity vs value

The headline signals mostly track observable activity, which is a weak proxy for outcomes. Activity is attractive precisely because it is instant, countable, and fully under the rep's control, while the things that actually matter (a closed deal, an expanded account, a renewed contract) arrive late and depend on factors no single rep owns. The dashboard therefore drifts toward what is convenient to count rather than what is worth counting.

Why proxies drift

Goodhart's Law

When a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure. Once "calls logged" is the number a rep is judged on, the rational move is to maximize calls, not to advance deals, so the metric decouples from the outcome it was meant to predict. Leading indicators of effort get read as if they were lagging indicators of value.

Frameworks applied

I separated the operational drivers of performance from the outcomes they are meant to predict using three lenses. Leading versus lagging indicators made explicit that outreach is a leading signal and revenue a lagging one, and that the two cannot be scored on the same axis. Proxy-metric risk framed why a convenient stand-in degrades once it carries incentive weight. And the Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan and Norton) gave me a structure to hold financial, customer, internal-process, and learning perspectives together, so no single easy-to-count number gets to stand in for the whole picture.

The risks if unaddressed

Left in place, an activity-led scorecard creates three compounding risks. First, distorted strategy: the pipeline looks healthy because the activity counts are high, right up until the revenue does not arrive. Second, eroded customer relationships, as reps are nudged toward volume of touches over the trust and qualification that actually close enterprise deals. Third, a culture that rewards visible effort over results, which is self-reinforcing because the people who game the proxy look like the top performers.

The redesigned scorecard

Outcome metrics

Rebalance the dashboard around what value actually looks like: deal conversion rate, revenue, average deal size, and retention. These are lagging by nature, and that is the point.

Pipeline-quality metrics

Add signals that predict outcomes without collapsing into raw activity: qualification scores, probability-weighted pipeline value, deal velocity, and the count of genuinely engaged stakeholders.

Relationship outcomes

Instrument the durable side of value directly: retention, expansion revenue, and customer satisfaction, so the system rewards accounts that grow rather than just accounts that get touched.

The takeaway

My recommendation was to stop letting the dashboard treat effort as achievement. Keep the activity signals, but demote them to leading indicators that have to be validated against outcomes rather than celebrated on their own. The deeper lesson I took from this is that a metrics framework is itself a product decision: whatever you make visible and reward is what the organization will quietly optimize, so the honest move is to measure value and harm directly instead of measuring whatever happens to be easy to count.
