ResMail Package Crisis

ResMail is Northeastern's internal last-mile package system, built for 1990s letter mail but now acting as an under-automated micro-fulfillment center. I diagnosed it as not merely inefficient but structurally obsolete, and redesigned it into a digitally enabled, distributed, surge-resilient network.

My role
Product Manager
Context
Northeastern last-mile package operations, INFO 7374 Supply Chain Challenge
Team
3-person team

The problem

A system designed for 1990s letter mail is now absorbing modern parcel volume it was never built for. The symptoms are acute: 30 to 45 minute pickup lines, parcels overflowing into fire exits, perishables like medication and food spoiling in the backlog, and staff burnout driving turnover and overtime, alongside public complaints and lost items. I framed the verdict plainly: ResMail is not inefficient, it is structurally obsolete.

The diagnosis

Structurally obsolete, not inefficient

This is not a system that needs to work harder, it is a system built for the wrong job. ResMail was designed for 1990s letter mail and is now operating as an under-automated micro-fulfillment center, so no amount of effort at a single counter fixes a design that lacks the information and facilities backbone modern parcel volume requires.

Who it's for

The people on both sides of the counter: students waiting on time-sensitive packages, including medication and perishables, and the ResMail staff carrying the strain through long lines, overtime, and turnover. I balanced relieving immediate pressure on staff against restoring a reliable, fast pickup experience for recipients, with the framing that continued inaction risks a compliance, safety, or reputational event escalating to University Operations leadership.

Research & discovery

I analyzed the operation through SCOR (Plan, Source, Make, Deliver, Return) and the six supply-chain drivers: facilities, inventory, transportation, information, sourcing, and pricing. That lens showed the failure was not a single bottleneck but a missing information and facilities backbone: no warehouse management system, no RFID tracking, and a single centralized pickup point absorbing every surge. I then mapped where IT logistics tooling, WMS, RFID, IoT, and smart lockers, could carry load the manual process could not.

The solution

I redesigned ResMail into a digitally enabled, distributed, surge-resilient network. The point was to replace one overloaded counter with a network that scales with load, built on four interlocking moves rather than a single fix.

The four redesign moves

Central intake & sort hub

A single backbone running a warehouse management system plus RFID, so every parcel is tracked from arrival and the operation finally has the information layer it was missing.

Distributed pickup

Smart lockers and micro-staging near dorm clusters spread retrieval across campus, so surges no longer funnel into one queue at one window.

Intelligent prioritization

Routing that fast-tracks perishables, high-value, and urgent items, so medication and food stop sitting in an undifferentiated backlog.

Predictive surge staffing

Staffing sized to forecast demand peaks rather than to yesterday's averages, so labor is present before the surge rather than after it.

What I'd measure

Under 5 minpickup wait, from 30 to 45 min
Near zeropackage misplacement
Zerofire-code overflow
Lowerovertime hours

The takeaway

The headline target was pickup wait time from 30 to 45 minutes down to under 5 minutes, with package misplacement driven to near zero. I treated the wait time, overflow, overtime, and student satisfaction together as proof the network is sustainable and not just faster on a good day. The lesson I carried out of this is that when a system is structurally obsolete, the responsible move is to re-architect it through frameworks like SCOR and the six supply-chain drivers, not to keep asking the same counter to absorb more load.
