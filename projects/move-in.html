Move-In OS

A connected ecosystem for Northeastern move-in, where 4,500-plus students arrive within a 72-hour window across 40-plus residence halls with different layouts. I took a systems-driven approach to coordination, efficiency, and student experience rather than treating it as a one-day logistics scramble.

The problem

More than 4,500 students arrive within a single 72-hour window, spread across 40-plus residence halls that each have a different layout. That concentration of arrivals, vehicles, and volunteers means small coordination gaps cascade into long waits and gridlock. I framed the work around orchestrating people, space, and timing across many distinct buildings under one hard, shared deadline.

The framing

A connected ecosystem, not a one-day scramble

I treated move-in as a coordination system rather than a logistics event. When 4,500-plus arrivals compress into 72 hours across 40-plus buildings with different layouts, the right unit of design is the whole network of people, space, and timing, because a gap in one hall becomes gridlock for the next.

Who it's for

The work serves the 4,500-plus arriving students and their families who want a smooth first day, alongside the staff and volunteers running the operation across 40-plus halls. My aim was a connected ecosystem that reduces friction for both the served and the serving sides at once, rather than optimizing one at the expense of the other.

Research & discovery

I modeled the arrival flow across the 72-hour window and the 40-plus halls, identifying likely chokepoints such as parking, unloading, and elevator access that vary hall by hall because the layouts differ. Treating it as a system rather than a single venue, I explored where staggered scheduling, signage, and shared coordination could smooth demand and prevent congestion before it formed.

The solution

I framed a connected ecosystem rather than a static plan. The intent was to coordinate 40-plus distinct buildings as one system, spreading demand over time and keeping movement continuous rather than bursty, built on three coordinated levers.

The coordination levers

Arrival scheduling

Spread the 4,500-plus arrivals deliberately across the 72-hour window, so load is smoothed over time instead of spiking and stalling at the same hours.

Chokepoint staffing

Concentrate staff and volunteers at each hall's specific bottlenecks, since parking, unloading, and elevator constraints vary building by building.

Clear wayfinding

Signage and guidance so every family always knows the next step, keeping movement continuous and preventing confusion from turning into congestion.

What I'd measure

I would treat average wait and unload time, plus peak congestion at chokepoints across the 40-plus halls as the operational signals of success, watching whether load actually stayed spread across the full 72-hour window. I would pair that with student, family, and volunteer feedback so I am measuring experience quality, the real point of a systems-driven approach, and not just throughput.

The takeaway

The lesson I took from this is that high-stakes events compressed into a fixed window are won or lost on coordination, not effort. By designing move-in as a connected ecosystem across 40-plus halls rather than a one-day scramble, the work could smooth demand over the full 72 hours and serve the arriving and the serving sides at the same time, instead of trading one against the other.
