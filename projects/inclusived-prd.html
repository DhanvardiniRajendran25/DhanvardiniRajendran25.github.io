02 · Executive summary

Online learning was built for one kind of brain.

Between 15 and 20% of people are neurodivergent, yet only about 12% of learners complete the online courses they start. InclusivEd treats that gap as a cognitive-design failure, not a motivation problem. It adds four on-platform capabilities, adjustable display, content chunking, spaced-repetition flash cards, and relaxation moments, tuned to each learner by an accessibility profile and a lightweight personalization model.

The product bet: if the platform itself reduces cognitive load and adapts to how a learner’s brain works, completion rises without lowering rigor. Success is judged on one number, Course Completion Rate, watched most closely for neurodivergent learners, with counter-metrics that stop simplification from hollowing out the material.

03 · Background & context

Why this, and why now.

Coursera and its peers assume a linear, neurotypical learning path: sustained attention for 20 to 30 minutes, content read top to bottom, and strong self-management. Many learners’ brains do not work that way, so they stitch together browser extensions, planners, and text-to-speech tools just to finish one course.

The market case is direct. Neurodivergent learners are a large, underserved segment, accessibility expectations and legal compliance pressure are rising, and AI personalization is finally cheap enough to deliver per-learner adaptation on platform. A SWOT done in the case study reads this as Coursera’s opportunity to own: global brand trust and a deep content library on the strength side, a one-size-fits-all UX and high dropout on the weakness side.

Market sizing

TAM $690M (global Coursera revenue), SAM $120M (global neurodivergent e-learners), SOM $12M (US neurodivergent e-learners). Serving this segment well could grow the addressable base by roughly a fifth.

04 · Problem statement & evidence

A cognitive mismatch, measured.

Problem: the platform assumes a single, neurotypical way of learning, and the gap between that assumption and a neurodivergent learner’s reality is where completion dies. Three pain points recur: cognitive overload, a lack of personalization, and difficulty holding focus and attention, which compound into frustration, disengagement, and lower completion.

05 · Target users, personas & jobs to be done

Three learners I designed for.

The primary users are neurodivergent learners with ADHD, dyslexia, or autism. A third persona, the specialist and advocate, is the institutional buyer who recommends inclusive platforms. Each persona carries a job to be done framed as the outcome they are hiring the product for.

Segment shape

Within the neurodivergent population the rough split is: other neurodivergence ~78%, ADHD ~10%, dyslexia ~10%, autism ~3%. The product is designed for the shared cognitive needs across these groups, not a single diagnosis.

The MVP is an interactive prototype inside a real Coursera guided-project shell: a style switcher (OpenDyslexic, 12pt), flash cards, a dynamic display, and a relaxation-mode toggle. Prioritization is grounded in the idea-validation survey, the most-wanted accommodations ship first.

Kano framing

Basics: Content Chunking and Display Flexibility, expected, and their absence frustrates. Performance: Flash Cards, more is better, they scale with effort. Delighter: Relaxation Moments, unexpected, and disproportionately loved when present. The MVP ships the Basics first and layers the Delighter last.

10 · AI capability & system behavior

How the AI behaves, and what it never does.

Three AI capabilities sit under the four features: a personalization and recommendation layer that maps the accessibility profile and in-session signals to display, pacing, and break suggestions; a text-to-speech service over content and cards; and a spaced-repetition scheduler that decides which items to rehearse and when.

AI behavior contractAI.md

Inputs, outputs, and guardrails

Inputs: accessibility profile (display, pacing, rehearsal prefs), in-session signals (replays, pauses, dwell time, quiz misses), course content structure, and the daily check-in answer.
Outputs: a display configuration, a chunking layout, an ordered flash-card queue with due times, and an optional relaxation-moment suggestion, each presented as a suggestion the learner accepts or dismisses.
Never: infer or display a medical diagnosis; force a break or mode change; alter the meaning of instructor content; rank or compare learners; lock a learner out of plain defaults.

Behavioral rules

  • Suggest, never seize: the AI proposes adaptations; the learner stays in control of every change.
  • Presentation, not content: it reshapes how material is shown and rehearsed, it does not rewrite what the instructor taught.
  • Graceful fallback: if a signal is missing or a model call fails, the system falls back to the saved profile, then to plain platform defaults.
  • Transparent rehearsal: flash cards are drawn from the learner’s own course content, never invented facts.

Privacy stance: the accessibility profile is self-declared, never inferred as a diagnosis. Engagement signals drive adaptation in session and are minimized at rest. No learner data is used to label, rank, or compare individuals, and a learner can export or delete their profile.

12 · Model & personalization strategy

Start simple, earn complexity.

Personalization runs in tiers so the product is useful on day one and improves with data, without ever depending on a black box to function.

01

Rules first

The accessibility profile deterministically sets display, chunking, and TTS defaults. This alone delivers the Basics.

02

Signals layer

In-session signals (replays, pauses, quiz misses) nudge pacing and trigger optional relaxation suggestions.

03

Spaced repetition

A scheduling model orders the flash-card queue by recall difficulty and time since last seen.

04

Recommendation

A lightweight model proposes display and pacing adaptations, always as accept-or-dismiss suggestions.

Every tier degrades cleanly to the one below it. If the recommendation model is unavailable, signals still nudge; if signals are missing, the saved profile still applies; if the profile is empty, plain platform defaults take over.

13 · UX & accessibility requirements

Accessibility is the floor, not a feature.

  • WCAG: the base UI meets WCAG 2.1 AA, contrast, keyboard navigation, focus order, and screen-reader semantics, before any adaptation layers on top.
  • OpenDyslexic and readable type: a font switcher includes OpenDyslexic, with adjustable size (down to and including 12pt presets), letter and line spacing, and color and contrast themes.
  • Focus modes: a focused mode hides non-essential UI and surfaces one task at a time; a relaxation moment offers a calm, low-stimulation break.
  • Consistency: a stable, predictable layout for pattern-seeking learners, with checklists and progress bars; the same content, reflowed, never relabeled.
  • No dark patterns: every adaptation is reversible, opt-out is always one tap away, and nothing is forced on the learner.

14 · Evaluation framework

How we will know it worked.

InclusivEd has generative parts, auto-generated lesson summaries, spaced-repetition flash cards, and text-to-speech, so evaluation has two jobs. First, prove the product outcome: more neurodivergent learners finish. Second, prove the generated artifacts are trustworthy: a summary that distorts the lesson or a flash card that teaches the wrong fact is worse than none. The framework below combines a human rubric, automated checks, and an LLM-as-a-judge for the generated text, all benchmarked against a baseline and tracked per neurotype. These are target thresholds for a pilot, not achieved results.

Evaluation dataset

The offline evaluation set is a fixed sample of real Coursera lessons paired with their generated summaries and flash cards, balanced across the three neurotype cohorts so no single profile dominates the scores.

Human-evaluation rubric

Trained reviewers, including neurodivergent learners, score every generated artifact in the set against a fixed rubric. Each dimension is rated on a defined scale so two reviewers land in the same place.

DimensionDefinition
Content fidelityThe summary or card reflects the lesson faithfully, with no added, dropped, or distorted facts.
ReadabilityPlain, well-chunked language a learner in the target profile can read without re-reading.
Comprehension liftWhether the artifact plausibly helps a learner understand and retain the material, judged against the source.
Accessibility complianceOutput respects WCAG semantics, contrast, reading order, and works with assistive technology.
Summary qualityThe summary is complete enough to stand in for a first read, concise, and free of over-simplification.
Flashcard qualityEach card tests one real idea from the lesson, with a correct and unambiguous answer.

Automated evaluators

Cheap, deterministic checks run on every generated artifact before it ever reaches a human reviewer, so regressions are caught fast and at scale.

  • Grounding and overlap: source-overlap and entailment checks confirm each summary sentence and card is supported by the actual lesson text.
  • Readability scoring: automated readability indices flag output that drifts above the target reading level for a profile.
  • Accessibility linting: automated WCAG checks on contrast, heading order, and structure on every render.
  • Structural validity: flash cards must parse into a clean question and answer pair; malformed cards are rejected, not shown.

LLM as a judge

LLM as a judge · generated summaries & flash cards

For the auto-generated summaries and flash cards, an LLM is used as a judge: it scores each summary for fidelity to the source lesson (did it distort, add, or drop meaning) and each flash card for quality (one real idea, correct answer, no hallucination), against a fixed rubric and the lesson as ground truth. This scales the human rubric across thousands of artifacts cheaply and consistently.

The LLM judge has real limits and is treated as a screen, not a verdict:

  • Bias: the judge can inherit the same blind spots as the generator and may favor fluent text over correct text.
  • Needs human and learner validation: judge scores are sampled and confirmed by human reviewers and by neurodivergent learners; the judge never auto-approves an artifact on its own.
  • Cannot measure real comprehension: it scores the text, not whether a learner actually understood or retained the material; only the pilot completion and recall data can speak to that.

Regression, segment-level & production evaluation

  • Regression testing: the frozen evaluation set is re-scored on every model or prompt change; a drop on fidelity, readability, or accessibility blocks the release.
  • Segment-level evaluation (fairness): every metric is broken out across the ADHD, dyslexia, and autism cohorts so a gain for one group cannot hide a loss for another; a per-cohort regression is treated as a release blocker.
  • Production sampling: a continuous random sample of live summaries and cards is run back through the automated checks and the LLM judge, with a slice routed to human review, so quality is watched after launch, not just before it.

Trust, grounding & change management

Because the summaries and flash cards are generated, the product is built to stay honest about what it produced and to keep a human in the loop.

SafeguardHow it works
Confidence & uncertainty handlingWhen the grounding or judge score for a summary is low, the artifact is flagged as low-confidence: the learner sees a clear note and the source lesson is shown alongside so they can verify before relying on it.
Hallucination managementEvery summary and card is grounded in the actual lesson text and gated by the grounding check; ungrounded or unsupported output is suppressed rather than shown, never invented facts.
Human in the loopLearners can correct or flag any summary or card inline; flags route into the production-sampling review queue and feed prompt and model fixes.
Monitoring & observabilityGenerated artifacts are traceable: each one logs its model version and prompt version alongside its grounding and judge scores, so a quality dip can be traced to a specific change.
Change managementModel and prompt changes are versioned and must clear the frozen regression set and segment-level fairness read before rollout, with a fast rollback to the prior version if a guardrail trips.

15 · Safety, privacy & security

Handling sensitive learner data with care.

  • No diagnosis: the product never infers or stores a medical diagnosis; the accessibility profile is self-declared and editable.
  • FERPA-aware: learner education records are treated as protected; access is scoped, logged, and consistent with FERPA expectations for student data.
  • Data minimization: engagement signals drive in-session adaptation and are minimized at rest; profiles are exportable and deletable on request.
  • Accessibility compliance: the experience is held to recognized accessibility standards, and a fix for one group is regression-tested against the others.
  • Human control: the learner can disable any AI behavior and fall back to plain platform defaults at any time.

16 · Analytics & experimentation

Measuring without optimizing the wrong thing.

  • Headline: Course Completion Rate, segmented for neurodivergent learners, tracked against a holdout control.
  • Experiment design: pilot a handful of courses with SmartAssist on, measure completion and retention against the standard experience.
  • Feature attribution: per-feature accept and usage rates, so the team can tell which of the four features actually move completion.
  • Guardrails in the dashboard: cognitive load, external-tool reliance, and accessibility regressions sit beside the headline so simplification cannot quietly hollow out rigor.
  • Vanity, flagged as vanity: average time with SmartAssist enabled is reported but explicitly not an optimization target.

18 · Edge cases & fallbacks

When things are missing or fail.

  • Empty profile: no preferences set, fall back to plain platform defaults; offer the daily check-in to seed a profile.
  • Sparse signals: too little in-session data, hold the saved profile rather than guessing pacing changes.
  • Model unavailable: recommendation service down, deterministic rules still drive display, chunking, and rehearsal.
  • TTS failure: speech service unavailable, content stays fully readable and the TTS control degrades quietly.
  • Content not chunkable: unstructured media that cannot be split, present it intact rather than mis-chunking it.
  • Conflicting accommodations: a setting that helps one need but hurts another, surface the trade-off and let the learner choose.
