MBTA Student Pass

A flexible student transit pass for Greater Boston, where 250,000+ students across 50+ universities are served by options that do not fit them. The case pairs personas and jobs-to-be-done with a TAM near $225M and a three-way stakeholder map.

The market

Greater Boston is the country's student capital, and that scale is the whole opportunity. The problem is not demand; it is that the existing fare products were never designed for how students actually move.

250,000+students in Greater Boston
50+universities
~162kstudents in Boston proper
~$225MTAM, 250k x ~$900/yr transit

The problem

Today's options fail the people they are meant to serve. The Semester Pass offers only an 11% discount, runs around $320, and is non-pausable and non-refundable, so a student riding three times a week pays roughly $230 in fares yet $320 for the pass. The Youth Pass carries narrow eligibility and heavy paperwork. University subsidies are uneven, and the CharlieCard, mTicket, and contactless ecosystem confuses new arrivals. The rigid semester model ignores co-op entirely, while the MBTA itself faces fraud and unpredictable co-op ridership.

Personas and jobs-to-be-done

I grounded the strategy in distinct rider types, each with a different job-to-be-done that the current products cannot satisfy.

Daily Commuter

Rides constantly and wants a predictable unlimited pass at or under $90 a month, with no surprises.

Occasional Rider

Travels irregularly and wants fare capping, so light use never costs more than a pass would.

Co-op Student

Moves in and out of full-time work and wants to pause or swap within 24 hours, with pro-rated credit.

International / New Arrival

Lands without local fare setup and wants to tap and ride from day one, no paperwork first.

Low-Income Rider

Qualifies automatically through Pell eligibility, removing the application friction that gates today's discounts.

The strategy

I framed a flexible pass around the trade-off between adoption, per-ride economics, and long-term habit formation, designing the structure to fit each persona's job rather than forcing one rigid semester product on all of them. Fare capping protects occasional riders, pause-and-swap with pro-rating respects the co-op calendar, tap-and-ride lowers the barrier for new arrivals, and automatic Pell eligibility removes paperwork for low-income students. The intent throughout was to lower the barrier for students while protecting the agency on unit economics.

Why everyone wins

The concept only holds if it serves three constituencies at once, so I mapped the value each stakeholder gets from the same pass.

Universities

Gain subsidy dashboards and reduced parking demand, turning a vague benefit into something measurable.

The MBTA

Earns more predictable revenue and reduced fraud, smoothing the unpredictable co-op ridership that strains planning today.

City and state

See fewer single-occupancy vehicles, advancing congestion and climate goals at the same time.

The core insight

STRATEGIC FRAME

The pass should flex to the rider, not the other way around. Students riding three times a week pay roughly $230 in fares but $320 for a non-pausable pass, so the design failure is rigidity, and flexibility is the product.

What I'd measure

I would define success through net-new ridership and student adoption rate rather than raw pass sales, paired with revenue-per-rider to guard against cannibalization. I would track pause-and-swap usage among co-op students as a signal the flexibility is doing its job, and watch retention into non-discounted ridership over time, since the long-term bet is that an affordable student pass builds a transit habit that outlasts graduation.
