Husky Happs

A campus events app that aggregates every university and club event into one feed for undergraduate and graduate students.

My role
Product Manager: problem framing, scoping, and feature definition
Context
Product concept & design · Northeastern University
Users
Undergraduate and graduate students across the campus

The problem

There is no shortage of things to do on a campus the size of Northeastern's. The problem is finding out about them in time. Events live in scattered places: department emails, printed flyers, individual club pages, separate sign-up portals, and group chats. By the time a student hears about something interesting, it has often already happened. Two groups lose out at once: students miss events they would have loved, and clubs that put real effort into planning struggle to reach the people who would actually show up.

Who it is for

Husky Happs is built for the whole student body, and I was deliberate about including graduate students, not just undergraduates. The two groups have genuinely different rhythms and interests: a grad student is rarely looking for the same event as a first-year, and a feed that ignores that difference quickly becomes noise. Designing for both from the start meant relevance had to be a first-class concern rather than an afterthought.

Discovery

Listening to how students actually keep up with campus life, the pattern was consistent: discovery is fragmented, and the burden falls entirely on the student to go hunting across ten different places. The events that get attendance are usually the ones a student happens to already follow, which means good events with the wrong reach simply disappear. The core job to be done was simple to state and hard to solve well: show me what is worth my time on campus, in one place, without me having to chase it.

The idea

CONCEPT

One feed for everything happening on campus. Husky Happs aggregates every university and club event, with full details, into a single filterable feed built for both undergraduate and graduate students, so discovery becomes one place instead of ten.

The build

Unified event feed

Every university and club event flows into one place, so students stop checking a dozen sources and clubs stop competing for attention across fragmented channels.

Full event detail

Each listing carries the details that decide whether someone actually goes: what, when, where, who it is for, and how to take part, so a student can commit without leaving the app.

Relevance filters

Filters by audience and interest keep undergraduate and graduate views distinct, so the feed reads as a curated list rather than an undifferentiated firehose.

Publish once, reach everyone

Clubs post a single time and reach the right students automatically, which removes the manual cross-posting that limited their reach before.

Key decisions

The most important decision was to treat relevance, not raw volume, as the product's real value. It would have been easy to dump every event into one stream and call it aggregation, but that just recreates the noise problem inside a single app. By making audience and interest filtering central, I scoped the feed to surface fewer, better-matched events per student. I also chose to serve both the student side and the club side of the marketplace, because a discovery app with no incentive for organizers to publish would slowly starve of content.

What I would measure

Since this is a concept build, I held back from inventing numbers and instead defined what success would look like. The signals I would watch first: the share of students who find at least one event per week worth attending, repeat use as a habit rather than a one-time look, and on the club side, whether publishing once measurably widens an event's reach versus the old scatter of channels. A healthy product here would show students discovering things they would otherwise have missed, which is exactly the gap I set out to close. The takeaway: aggregation alone is not the product, relevance is.
