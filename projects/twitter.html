Twitter's Engagement Trap

When engagement is not enough: a diagnosis of the activity-versus-value gap at Twitter, where easy-to-measure engagement signals were treated as proof of value, and a uniform Agile model quietly starved the work that protects the platform.

My role
Product Manager
Context
Solo analysis · activity-versus-value critique (Northeastern, INFO 7374)
Focus
Metrics design · org & delivery model

Two jobs, one feed

Twitter launched in 2006 as a real-time public information network, structurally different from Facebook's private social graph. Through the Value Proposition Canvas, its promise was immediacy, openness, and reliable access to information. But the Jobs-to-be-Done lens exposes a split: users hired Twitter for an informational job (stay current, hear diverse perspectives, join public discourse), while the product increasingly optimized for a different job, keeping users on-platform long enough to serve ads. The gap between those two jobs is where the activity-versus-value problem begins.

Measuring the wrong thing well

As Twitter scaled, tweet volume, retweets, impressions, DAU, mDAU, and time-on-platform became embedded in roadmaps, algorithm calibration, and investor communications. Borrowing Parmenter's distinction, these were Result Indicators that merely report activity, not KPIs causally connected to platform health. Engagement signals were, at best, lagging proxies for value, but they were treated as leading indicators, systematically steering investment toward what was easy to measure and away from what was not.

The core distinction

Engagement as a leading value indicator

The platform read engagement as if it caused value, when it only correlated with attention. A metric that goes up when people scroll is not the same as a metric that goes up when the network stays trustworthy and safe, yet the first was instrumented everywhere and the second was barely instrumented at all.

Agile as an amplifier

An Agile-dominant engineering culture compounded the bias. Agile's strength, short feedback cycles and ship-and-learn, fits features whose value is observable within a sprint: an algorithm tweak's effect on retweets shows up in days. Trust-and-safety infrastructure does not. As Boehm and Turner argue, capabilities whose value is measured in harm prevented, whose failures are catastrophic rather than incremental, favor traditional, long-horizon delivery. Applying one uniform Agile model to the entire stack made fast, measurable engagement wins structurally favored over slow, hard-to-attribute safety work, not by intent, but as a predictable output of the delivery model.

Stakeholders who defined success differently

The measurement problem persisted partly because Twitter's stakeholder ecosystem held structurally incompatible definitions of success. Mapping them with Mendelow's matrix, advertisers, investors, power users, everyday users, and regulators each rewarded a different signal, and the loudest, most quantifiable definitions (engagement, time-on-platform) drowned out the diffuse ones (informational trust, safety, civic health). No neutral arbiter reconciled them, so the most countable definition won by default.

The proposition

Separate KPIs from result indicators

Read engagement as a driver to be validated against platform health, not an outcome to be celebrated, so the scorecard stops mistaking attention for value.

Instrument value and harm directly

Give informational-trust and safety their own metrics that survive on a dedicated scorecard, instead of forcing them to compete for sprint attention against fast engagement wins.

Match delivery model to the work

Ring-fence long-horizon trust-and-safety investment from the fast engagement loop, so neither capability is judged by the other's clock.

The takeaway

What I took from this is that the activity-versus-value gap is rarely a measurement accident. It is the joint output of which signals are easy to count, which delivery model ships them fastest, and which stakeholders shout loudest. Fixing it means changing all three at once: separate the indicators, instrument the slow value directly, and protect the long-horizon work from being scored on the engagement loop's timeline.
