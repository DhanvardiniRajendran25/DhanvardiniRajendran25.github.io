TSMC Geographic Expansion

TSMC makes more than 90% of the world's advanced logic chips, almost entirely on one island, and is now pouring $165B into US fabs to diversify. My finding: this is the supply concentration paradox, distributing the Make process does not de-risk Source, it just moves the risk upstream.

Product Manager
Framework
SCOR: Plan, Source, Make, Deliver, Enable
Context
TSMC geographic expansion: Arizona, Kumamoto, Dresden
Scope

The situation

TSMC manufactures more than 90% of the world's leading-edge logic chips, and historically nearly all of that capacity sat in Taiwan. This is the largest geographic expansion in semiconductor history: a $165B US commitment for six fabs plus packaging and R&D, up from a $12B start in 2020, alongside builds in Kumamoto, Japan and Dresden, Germany, backed by $6.6B in US CHIPS Act subsidies. In 2025 TSMC posted $122.4B in revenue, up 35.9%, even as Arizona Fab 1 turned a small profit while Kumamoto absorbed a NT$6.2B loss in the same period.

The scale of the bet

This is the largest geographic expansion in semiconductor history, and the numbers are the reason the question is hard: the dependency being diversified is enormous, and so is the price of diversifying it.

The core question

Can the most complex manufacturing process on earth be replicated across continents without losing what makes it work? I analyzed this through the SCOR model (Plan, Source, Make, Deliver, Enable), asking whether spreading out the Make process genuinely de-risks supply, or whether it just pays a large premium to relocate a problem the underlying ecosystem will not let you move. The early evidence is mixed: Arizona Fab 1 turned a small net profit (NT$4.7B) in early 2025 while JASM Kumamoto absorbed a NT$6.2B loss in the same period, which tells you the relocation is real but far from free.

The thesis

The supply concentration paradox

Distributing Make does not de-risk Source. In SCOR terms, TSMC diversified the Make process across continents while the Source layer stays single-sourced, so the risk is moved upstream, not eliminated.

My central finding is that the most critical inputs stay concentrated no matter where the fab sits. EUV lithography comes only from ASML in the Netherlands, precision optics only from Zeiss in Germany, and specialty chemicals from a handful of suppliers. A single ASML EUV machine costs roughly $200M, weighs 180 tons, and ships in 40 freight containers, and there is no second vendor. You can build a fab in Arizona, but it still runs on a Dutch machine full of German optics. The geopolitical and single-point-of-failure exposure does not disappear, it relocates one tier up the chain.

The tensions beyond cost

The roughly 50% cost premium and 4-to-5x construction bills are only the visible price. Several deeper tensions decide whether the expansion actually delivers resilience.

Yield maturity

Overseas fabs face open questions on quality and yield that Taiwan's mature lines have long since answered.

Innovation speed

Colocation in Taiwan's science parks beats distributed coordination, which adds latency between Make and the R&D that feeds it.

Government KPIs

CHIPS Act subsidies ($6.6B US, $7.8B Japan, EUR 5B EU) attach job and buyback guardrails, and a proposed N-2 rule could keep overseas fabs two generations behind.

Resource load

Each fab draws around 15 million gallons of water a day, and the US needs roughly 115,000 semiconductor workers by 2030.

Tradeoffs: who is buying what

Customers and governments are effectively buying insurance through subsidies, accepting roughly 50% higher unit cost and 4-to-5x construction bills in exchange for sovereignty and continuity of supply. That is a coherent trade for a strategic input, but it is important to name it as one. The most advanced R&D and leading nodes remain anchored in Taiwan, so what the expansion buys today is partial redundancy, not true independence: a second place to make slightly older chips, on machines that still come from exactly one place.

My recommendation

I frame this to stakeholders as resilience as a paid option, not a free lunch. The premium is justified for an input this strategic, but only if you measure progress by the right things: node parity with Taiwan and genuine Source-side ecosystem depth abroad, not by ribbon-cuttings and fab counts. If the leading node and the upstream toolchain both stay in one geography, you have paid for the appearance of diversification while keeping the substance of concentration.

My takeaway

My verdict is that efficiency and resilience can coexist, sort of, but it is painful and expensive, and the geopolitical risk justifies the premium. The durable lesson for me as a PM is to follow the dependency all the way up: de-risking the step everyone can see (the fab) while ignoring the step nobody can move (EUV, optics, chemicals) is a very expensive way to feel safer without being safer. Until that upstream base diversifies too, the concentration risk is mitigated, not solved.
