Overview

A cognitive-accessibility layer for Coursera.

InclusivEd, surfaced in the product as SmartAssist, is an in-platform accessibility layer for the 15 to 20% of learners who are neurodivergent: ADHD, dyslexia, and autism. Only about 12% of learners finish the online courses they start, and I treated that gap as a cognitive-design failure, not a motivation problem. Rather than build a sixth external tool on top of the planners, extensions, and text-to-speech apps learners already stitch together, InclusivEd makes the platform itself adapt how a course looks, paces, and rehearses.

I led this as the Product Manager on a 4-person team. I owned the research, personas, prioritization, metric definition, and the end-to-end AI PRD. The bet: if the platform reduces cognitive load and adapts to how a learner’s brain works, completion rises without lowering rigor.

Vision & success metrics

Learning that adapts to how your brain works.

North Star metric · CCR

Confident Completion Rate (CCR), the share of enrolled learners who actually finish a course, watched most closely for neurodivergent learners. It pairs completion with confidence: finishing without burning out or abandoning halfway.

CCR rests on the principles that anchored the work: universal design, platform-level support, a strengths-based stance, and seamless integration. The metric tree keeps it honest.

North Star & primary

  • Course completion rate the core outcome
  • Neurodivergent completion lift the primary audience
  • Module-to-module retention momentum, not just starts
  • Time-to-first-win early momentum builds confidence

Counter · guardrail

  • Cognitive load per session simplification can’t hollow out rigor
  • Drop-off points where learners still fall behind
  • External-tool reliance still needing 5 apps means we failed
  • Accessibility regressions a fix for one group can hurt another

Qualitative · signals

  • Dignity “this was built for me”
  • Momentum small wins that compound
  • Clarity always knowing the next step
  • Belonging not “I’m just not smart enough”

Product strategy & positioning

An on-platform layer, not a sixth tool.

The strategic choice that shaped everything: InclusivEd lives inside Coursera, not beside it. Learners already juggle roughly five external tools to finish one course, so adding a sixth would deepen the problem. By making the platform itself adapt, I positioned InclusivEd as infrastructure Coursera owns, defensible behind its brand and content library, rather than a bolt-on a competitor could copy overnight.

Positioning statement

For neurodivergent learners who start more courses than they finish, InclusivEd is an in-Coursera accessibility layer that adapts how a course looks, paces, and rehearses. Unlike browser extensions and standalone study apps, it works inside the learning experience the learner already trusts, and it reshapes presentation without rewriting what the instructor taught.

Universal design

Build for the shared cognitive needs across ADHD, dyslexia, and autism, not a single diagnosis.

Platform-level support

Kano framing

Basics: Content Chunking and Display Flexibility, expected, and their absence frustrates. Performance: Flash Cards, scaling with effort. Delighter: Relaxation Moments, unexpected and disproportionately loved. The plan ships the Basics first and layers the Delighter last.

MVP

The smallest thing that proves the bet.

The MVP is an interactive prototype built inside a real Coursera guided-project shell, so the experience is tested in context rather than in a mockup. It carries just enough to validate whether reducing cognitive load moves completion.

Style switcher

OpenDyslexic font and a 12pt preset, applied live to the lesson without reload.

Dynamic display

Adjustable layout, size, color, and contrast carried by a saved accessibility profile.

Flash cards

Bite-sized, spaced-repetition cards drawn from the module’s own content.

Relaxation-mode toggle

An optional guided break the learner can turn on, never forced.

Out of MVP scope by design: condition diagnosis, a standalone app or browser extension, and authoring net-new course content.

Technical architecture

The AI & personalization stack.

Personalization runs in tiers so the product is useful on day one and improves with data, without ever depending on a black box to function. Every tier degrades cleanly to the one below it.

01

Rules first

The self-declared accessibility profile deterministically sets display, chunking, and TTS defaults. This alone delivers the Basics.

02

Signals layer

In-session signals (replays, pauses, dwell time, quiz misses) nudge pacing and trigger optional relaxation suggestions.

03

Spaced repetition

A scheduling model orders the flash-card queue by recall difficulty and time since last seen.

04

Recommendation

A lightweight model proposes display and pacing adaptations, always as accept-or-dismiss suggestions.

Behavioral rules the AI follows

  • Suggest, never seize: the AI proposes adaptations; the learner stays in control of every change.
  • Presentation, not content: it reshapes how material is shown and rehearsed, never rewriting what the instructor taught.
  • Graceful fallback: if a signal is missing or a model call fails, the system falls back to the saved profile, then to plain platform defaults.
  • Grounded rehearsal: flash cards and summaries are drawn from the learner’s own course content, gated by a grounding check, never invented facts.

The PRD details the data domains, privacy stance (self-declared profile, FERPA-aware, minimized at rest), and the evaluation framework, including an LLM-as-a-judge screen for generated summaries and cards.

Learnings & retrospective

What this project taught me.

Reframe before you redesign

The biggest unlock was naming completion as a cognitive-design failure instead of a motivation problem. The reframe pointed every later decision at the platform, not the learner.

Let survey demand do the sequencing

Tying the feature order to what learners actually asked for (80% fonts, 75% chunking, 60% relaxation) made prioritization an evidence call, not an opinion.

One number keeps a team honest

Committing to Course Completion Rate, with counter-metrics beside it, stopped the work from chasing engagement time and forced simplification to prove it did not hollow out rigor.

Accessibility is a floor, not a feature

Designing for shared cognitive needs across ADHD, dyslexia, and autism, with WCAG as the baseline, was harder than a single-diagnosis fix but far more defensible.

What I would do next

  • Run the pilot on a handful of courses and measure Course Completion Rate against the holdout control, broken out per neurotype.
  • Usability-test the focused-mode and daily check-in flows with neurodivergent learners before widening scope.
  • Stand up the LLM-as-a-judge and grounding checks on generated summaries and cards before any of that content reaches a learner.
