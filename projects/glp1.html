GLP-1: When Supply Is the Product

A product-supply-chain integration analysis of the GLP-1 ecosystem, Novo Nordisk's Ozempic and Wegovy, where a product promise of uninterrupted therapeutic continuity ran headlong into a supply chain that could not scale fast enough. When the medicine must never stop, supply stops being logistics and becomes the experience itself.

Product
Novo Nordisk semaglutide: Ozempic (2017) and Wegovy (2021)

The promise is continuity

Ozempic (type 2 diabetes, 2017) and Wegovy (chronic weight management, 2021) are not one-time interventions. Patients initiate treatment, titrate through escalating doses over weeks, and then maintain a stable dose indefinitely. Any interruption directly undermines the outcome: weight regain on discontinuation is documented, and glycemic control deteriorates without sustained dosing. The product's value is inseparable from uninterrupted access, which makes supply reliability a feature, not a back-office concern.

The demand shock, in numbers

What turned a niche diabetes therapy into a national shortage was the shape of the demand curve, not its level. These figures are the reason a steady-state supply chain never stood a chance.

The thesis

When supply is the product

The vulnerability was structural, not accidental. An innovative product was being served by an efficient supply chain, and Fisher identifies exactly that mismatch as the root cause of supply failure.

In Fisher's (1997) framework, GLP-1 medications are an innovative product: unpredictable demand, high margin, short windows to acquire patients. Yet they were served by an efficient supply chain designed for the predictable, steady-state demand of traditional diabetes therapy. Because continuity is the product's promise, the mismatch did not just cost sales, it broke the experience itself: every stockout is a patient whose titration is interrupted.

Why interruption is the failure mode

Ozempic and Wegovy are not one-time interventions. Patients initiate, titrate through escalating doses over weeks, and then maintain a stable dose indefinitely. Any interruption directly undermines the outcome: weight regain on discontinuation is documented, and glycemic control deteriorates without sustained dosing. That is what makes supply reliability a feature rather than a back-office concern. A consumer product can ration scarce stock and merely annoy buyers; a chronic therapy that rations stock reverses the clinical result it was prescribed to produce.

Where it breaks: a tightly coupled chain

Injectable semaglutide moves through three sequential stages, and the failure is concentrated in the middle one.

API production

Yeast-based fermentation produces the active ingredient, a biological process that scales slowly and cannot be rushed on demand.

Fill-finish (the bottleneck)

Filling into a proprietary pen injection device is tightly coupled and slow to duplicate. This is the binding constraint where capacity cannot be added in weeks.

Cold-chain

Distribution at 2 to 8°C adds a temperature-controlled layer that limits buffering and routing flexibility downstream.

Applying Lee's (2002) work on the bullwhip effect and architecture lock-in, the proprietary fill-finish device is the choke point: demand variability amplifies upstream, and the chain's own architecture, not a forecasting error, is what concentrates the risk.

The proposition

Treating continuity as the product reframes the fixes into three moves. (1) Rematch chain to product: shift from a pure efficiency design toward responsive capacity (buffered API, parallel fill-finish lines, device second-sourcing) for the innovative-product phase. (2) Decouple the bottleneck: invest ahead of demand in fill-finish capacity rather than treating it as fixed, since that is the stage the architecture locks in. (3) Manage the patient promise explicitly: use titration-aware allocation and transparent continuity signals so that scarce supply protects existing patients' outcomes first.

Why allocation is a product decision

The third move is the one most easily mistaken for an operations footnote, and it is the one I would defend hardest. When supply is scarce, who gets the dose is a product decision, because it determines whose clinical outcome the product actually delivers. A new patient at the start of titration and a maintenance patient mid-course are not interchangeable demand units: cutting off the maintenance patient reverses a result already paid for. Titration-aware allocation turns a logistics rationing problem back into a question of keeping the product's core promise to the people who already depend on it.

My takeaway

GLP-1 is the case that taught me supply can be the product, not its plumbing. When the value proposition is uninterrupted continuity, the supply chain stops being a cost center to optimize and becomes the feature to design. As a PM, the discipline I take from it is to match the chain to the product's real demand profile before scaling, to invest ahead of the bottleneck the architecture will lock in, and to treat allocation under scarcity as a promise to existing users rather than a queue to be cleared.
