The problem

Today’s logins are barriers, not doors.

Passwords get forgotten. Fingerprints fail when hands are wet or worn. Face ID struggles with masks and poor lighting. For older adults and differently-abled users, these methods often feel like obstacles rather than solutions, and many depend on a caregiver just to set them up.

The opportunity is bigger than a fix: a more accessible, secure way to authenticate that empowers all users to interact safely and independently, and adapts to everyone, everywhere.

For older adults and differently-abled users this loop repeats across devices and sessions, costing them independence, dignity, and privacy. Heart ID’s job is to collapse the whole loop into wearing a watch.

Jobs-to-be-done

What users are really hiring authentication to do.

Underneath “unlock my device” sit deeper jobs about independence and dignity. I framed them as job stories so every feature could be traced back to a real motivation.

  • When I pick up my iPhone or open my Mac, I want it to recognize me without any action, so I can get in without fumbling, re-scanning, or asking for help.
  • When my hands are unsteady, worn, or occupied, I want a method that does not depend on them, so I can stay independent.
  • When I set up a new method, I want to do it once and have it just work across my devices, so I can avoid repeating calibration or relying on a caregiver.
  • When I authenticate in public, I want it to be invisible, so I can keep my dignity and privacy.

Product vision

Authentication becomes a state, not an action.

Vision

A world where access is something that simply happens while you wear your watch, never something you stop to perform. Identity is carried by the one biometric you cannot forget, leave at home, or hold wrong: your heartbeat. Designed accessibility-first, it works for the people other methods fail, and so it works better for everyone.

Apple’s authentication has moved steadily toward less friction: Passcode removed nothing, Touch ID removed the password, Face ID removed the touch. Each step still kept a moment of explicit action. The vision for Heart ID is to remove the action entirely, turning authentication from a discrete event into a continuous, ambient state.

Goal & success metrics

Authentication that disappears.

North Star metric · FAA

Frictionless Authenticated Access (FAA), the share of unlocks a user completes continuously and hands-free, without a password, a re-scan, or a caregiver. It captures the whole promise: secure access that simply happens while the watch is on the wrist.

One number isn’t enough for a product whose mandate is trust and inclusion, so FAA sits on a layered metric tree.

North Star & primary

  • % active devices on Heart ID adoption of the new method
  • Successful auth rate it works when you need it
  • Time spent authenticating ↓ reduction over time
  • Accessibility adoption rate the primary target audience

Counter · guardrail

  • Fallback auth usage high use means Heart ID is failing
  • Authentication failure rate false rejects erode trust fast
  • Battery impact on wearables continuous auth can’t drain the watch
  • Setup drop-off rate calibration must not lose people

Qualitative · signals

  • Independence users authenticate without a caregiver
  • Dignity no fumbling at a kiosk or in public
  • Confidence “it just knows it’s me”
  • Privacy the heart signature never leaves the secure enclave

Guardrails

Guardrails are the hard ceilings and floors that gate any release and trip alarms in the field. FAA can climb, but never by breaking one of these. These are targets for a 0 to 1 strategy, the bars the product must clear in the accessibility pilot, not achieved results.

The solution

Your heartbeat is the password.

The same concept in two voices: the product story, and how it actually works.

In plain language

Wear the watch, and you’re in.

Apple Heart ID learns the unique signature of your heartbeat once, during a short setup. After that, as long as your Apple Watch is on your wrist, your iPhone, iPad, and Mac recognize you and unlock, no passwords, no holding your face still, no re-scanning.

Because it’s continuous and hands-free, it works for the people other methods fail: someone with tremors, worn fingerprints, or low vision can use their devices independently and with dignity.

Under the hood

An ECG heart-signature biometric, processed on-device.

The Apple Watch’s ECG sensor captures the user’s cardiac pattern (ECG-HRV) and the Apple Neural Engine turns it into a private heart signature that is nearly impossible to replicate. Wrist detection gates the credential: take the watch off and the session locks.

The signature, created once via calibration, auto-syncs across Apple devices so a single enrollment unlocks the whole ecosystem. Fallback methods stay available, and the signature never leaves the secure enclave.

Technical architecture

How the signal becomes a session, all on-device.

The whole pipeline runs locally on hardware Apple already ships. The ECG signal is captured at the wrist, turned into a private embedding by the Neural Engine, matched against the enrolled template, and the result drives a session that propagates to paired devices. No raw waveform ever leaves the device.

CaptureWatch ECG sensor reads the cardiac signal and HRV
EmbedApple Neural Engine builds a private heart signature
Matchlive embedding compared to the enrolled template
↓
Gatewrist detection plus score threshold decide the session
Storesignature kept in the Secure Enclave, never the cloud
Propagateauthenticated state syncs to iPhone, iPad, Mac

Probabilistic core, deterministic gate

The matcher is probabilistic: a similarity score thresholded against a target. The gate is deterministic: wrist-off, revocation, or a sub-threshold score locks the session, no matter what.

On-device only

Capture, embedding, and matching all run on the Neural Engine. The template lives in the Secure Enclave, isolated from the OS and apps, and no raw ECG is transmitted.

Single enrollment, ecosystem reach

One calibration creates the signature, which auto-syncs securely so a verified watch unlocks every chosen device.

Key dependencies

Apple Watch ECG sensor, Apple Neural Engine, wrist detection, the Secure Enclave, and cross-device sync, all existing platform capabilities.

Product strategy & positioning

An accessibility-first wedge into a universal feature.

The strategy is deliberate sequencing: win the users that existing biometrics fail hardest, then expand to the whole base. The position is not “another biometric”; it is the first continuous, hands-free one, and the only consumer-grade ECG identity built into an ecosystem people already own.

Where we play

Inside Apple’s own ecosystem, on the wrist, anchored to the accessibility segment first and the broad base second.Beachhead: older adults and differently-abled users.

How we win

Pair hardware Apple already ships (ECG sensor, Neural Engine, wrist detection, Secure Enclave) with a design philosophy competitors cannot copy.Moat: ecosystem plus on-device privacy plus accessibility mandate.

What we believe

A passive, always-on biometric can serve the users passwords, fingerprint, and Face ID fail most while raising the security ceiling for everyone.Core bet: continuous beats discrete.

Positioned against the alternatives, Heart ID is the only method that is continuous and hands-free rather than a discrete moment of action. Passwords rely on memory, Touch ID and Face ID still demand a deliberate touch or glance, and passkeys move the secret but keep the moment. Heart ID removes the moment.

Minimum viable product

The smallest thing that proves the bet.

The MVP is exactly the MoSCoW “Must” cut: the continuous heart-signature core plus the accessibility path, nothing more. It is shippable as an accessibility pilot, and it is enough to validate the North Star (FAA) and the guardrails before any convenience extension is built.

Continuous heart-signature auth

The Watch ECG sensor and Neural Engine match a live signature against the enrolled template and hold an authenticated session while the watch stays on the wrist.

Guided, accessible calibration

A one-time setup with on-screen guidance and assistive-tech compatibility creates the signature, designed so the primary segment can complete it without a caregiver.

Cross-device sync + unlock

A single enrollment auto-syncs across iPhone, iPad, and Mac, so one calibration unlocks the chosen devices hands-free.

Wrist detection + fallback

Wrist-off invalidates the credential immediately, and passcode, Touch ID, or Face ID stay available so there is never a dead end.

Out of the MVP on purpose: assistive-tech deep integration, unrecognized-attempt alerts, Apple Pay and third-party app support, multi-user profiles, and low-battery mode all wait for later phases.

Key decisions & tradeoffs

What I chose, and what it cost.

Accessibility-first, not accessibility-also

Older adults and differently-abled users are the primary target, not an afterthought.Tradeoff: a narrower launch story, in exchange for a defensible wedge and a universal benefit.

Watch as the credential

The wrist is the one place a continuous, hands-free signal already lives.Tradeoff: requires an Apple Watch; addressed by the 60%+ iPhone-to-Watch ownership overlap.

Continuous + fallback

Wrist detection gates the credential; fallbacks stay for edge cases.Tradeoff: fallback usage is a counter-metric, watched closely.

On-device heart signature

Processed by the Neural Engine and kept in the secure enclave.Tradeoff: more on-device complexity, in exchange for privacy and trust.

Risks & mitigations

What could go wrong, and how I planned for it.

A continuous biometric carries real risk on both sides of the trade: too loose and it is insecure, too tight and it locks out the very users it is built for. Every risk below has a mitigation wired into the design or the metric tree.

RiskMitigation
Signal instability across conditions or health changesContinuous re-verification, threshold tuning, and easy re-calibration; never lock out, always fall back.
False accepts (security breach)Set the operating threshold to the FAR target first; wrist-off and revocation deterministically invalidate the credential.
False rejects (trust erosion)Tune FRR low, treat fallback usage as a watched counter-metric, and re-verify before any lock.
Battery drain from continuous authOn-device power profiling against a negligible-drain target, with a low-battery mode as a later option.
Requires an Apple WatchAddressed by the 60%+ iPhone-to-Watch ownership overlap and fallback on devices without a paired watch.
Privacy concern over heart dataOn-device processing, Secure Enclave storage, explicit health-data consent, and revocation.
Setup drop-off losing accessibility usersGuided, assistive-tech-compatible calibration; setup drop-off is a tracked guardrail.

Learnings & retrospective

What this project taught me as a PM.

Working with Protoneers, a 3-person team, I owned the product strategy end to end, from a validated problem to a measurable success framework. A few lessons stuck.

  • An accessibility-first wedge is a strategy, not a constraint. Anchoring to the users other methods fail produced a sharper position and a benefit that generalized to everyone, which made the broad case easier, not harder.
  • A North Star needs counter-metrics to stay honest. FAA only means something next to fallback usage, failure rate, battery, and a fairness gap; the guardrails are what keep a frictionless metric from hiding a broken experience.
  • For a biometric, the threshold is the product decision. Choosing to pin FAR to the security ceiling first, then tune FRR, shaped the whole experience; making it continuous let the operating point sit on the secure side without punishing real users.
  • Trust is designed, not assumed. On-device processing, the Secure Enclave, and a fairness release gate were product choices as much as engineering ones.

What I would do next

  • Run an accessibility pilot with older-adult and differently-abled users to pressure-test FAA and the failure-rate guardrail.
  • Prototype the calibration flow and measure real setup drop-off across segments.
  • Validate battery impact of continuous wrist-gated authentication against the under-2%-per-day guardrail.
