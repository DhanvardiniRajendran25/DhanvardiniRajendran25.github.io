IKEA Red Sea Disruption

When Red Sea attacks forced ships around Africa, IKEA held its price cuts and looked resilient. My finding: cost is not resilience. It held price only because raw-material deflation coincidentally offset a 3 to 4x freight spike. It got lucky. Price was a promise, and the disruption was a test it nearly failed.

My role
Product Manager
Frameworks
Value definition, stakeholder analysis, prioritization, Goodhart's Law
Context
IKEA / Ingka, Red Sea and Suez disruption, 2023 to 2024
Scope
EUR 47.6B Ingka revenue, ~1,600 suppliers across 50+ countries

The situation

Attacks on shipping in the Red Sea pushed carriers to divert around the Cape of Good Hope, adding roughly 10 to 14 days of transit (about 22 days stretching to 33 to 36) and sending 40-foot Asia-to-Europe ocean freight from around $1,500 to $2,000 in 2022 to roughly $6,000 to $8,000 by early 2024, a 3 to 4x spike. IKEA, the retail arm Ingka posting EUR 47.6B in FY2023 revenue across roughly 1,600 suppliers, leans heavily on exactly that lane. At Davos in January 2024 CEO Jesper Brodin described navigating on thinner profit, yet IKEA still held its price cuts.

The shock, in numbers

The Red Sea diversion was not a rounding error on the cost line, it was a multiple. These are the figures that frame why holding price was so notable, and why it was so precarious.

The thesis

Price as a promise, disruption as a test

Cost is not resilience. IKEA held its price cuts only because raw-material deflation coincidentally offset the freight spike. It got lucky, it was not structurally resilient.

If IKEA held its prices, was it actually resilient? I argue no. The right question is whether holding price reflected structural strength or a coincidence. IKEA defined value as consumer price level, but that is the wrong proxy for supply-chain health, so a stable shelf price masked a fragile underlying system. At Davos in January 2024 CEO Jesper Brodin described navigating on thinner profit, which is the tell: the price held, but the margin that absorbed the shock was being quietly eaten.

Analysis: four frameworks, one fragile system

I applied four course frameworks, and each pointed at the same blind spot: IKEA was measuring price while the real risk lived elsewhere.

Value definition

IKEA defined value as the consumer price level, a proxy that says nothing about supply-chain health. A stable shelf price can sit on top of a chain about to break.

Stakeholder analysis

Four groups matter (customers, ~1,600 suppliers, regulators and NGOs, the Ingka Foundation) but only customers are tracked closely. The rest go under-measured.

Prioritization

An efficiency-over-resilience trade-off, formalized as a self-reinforcing sourcing dependency loop that keeps choosing the cheaper, more concentrated option.

Goodhart's Law

Cost-per-unit became the target, so the team optimized the number while route concentration and buffer-coverage indicators stayed untracked entirely.

The dependency loop

The core mechanism is a self-reinforcing sourcing dependency loop: the price promise drives relentless low cost, which drives Asian manufacturing, which deepens Suez dependence. When disruption hits, IKEA absorbs it through inventory and margin, so nothing structural ever changes, and the loop resets stronger. Price held this time only because raw-material deflation happened to offset the 3-to-4x freight spike, not because the chain could flex. The loop is the reason "it got lucky" is the honest verdict: the structure never adapted, the macro environment simply blinked first.

Tradeoffs

This is the recurring efficiency versus resilience tradeoff, and IKEA has consistently chosen efficiency. Inventory and margin buffers smoothed the shock this time, but they are absorbers, not fixes, and the dependency loop guarantees the same exposure next time. The deeper trade is that a single-corridor, lowest-cost sourcing model is cheap until it isn't: when deflation does not show up to bail you out, the same structure that protected the price promise becomes the thing that breaks it.

My recommendation

My core lesson is that cost is not resilience, and the fix starts with what you measure. I recommend three moves, sequenced so measurement comes first. Balanced measurement: keep the cost dashboard but stand up a resilience dashboard tracking route concentration, a supplier geographic-diversity index, and buffer coverage. Break the dependency loop through targeted nearshoring, which IKEA has already begun in Poland, the Baltics, and Portugal. Harden the structure with route-flexible contracts carrying pre-negotiated Cape and Suez rates, a minimum buffer for Suez-exposed SKUs, and a formal resilience operating budget, so the next price promise is kept by design, not by luck.

My takeaway

What stays with me as a PM is how easily a healthy-looking metric hides a fragile system. IKEA optimized the one number customers could see and never built the instruments to see the risk that actually threatened it. The discipline I take forward is to pair every efficiency metric with a resilience metric, and to treat a stable outcome that depended on luck as a near-miss to be fixed, not a win to be celebrated. Price was a promise, the disruption was a test, and the only durable way to keep the promise is to measure the things that could break it.
