Recruiter Outreach App

I ran my own job search like a product: a local app that imports recruiter contacts, personalizes outreach, sends paced emails from Gmail, then tracks replies and auto-schedules follow-ups.

My role
Product Manager and builder: problem framing, workflow design, and the build itself
Context
Self-built local app · FastAPI + SQLite + Gmail API
User
A single power user (me), running a real job search

The problem

Job-search outreach is a leaky, manual funnel. You copy and paste the same email over and over, lose track of who replied, forget to follow up at the right moment, and never get a clear view of what is actually working. Doing it well by hand is genuinely a part-time job, and doing it badly quietly wastes good leads. I had real outreach to run, so rather than grind through it manually, I decided to treat my own search as a product and build the pipeline as software.

Who it is for

This is a tool with exactly one user, which shaped every decision. Because it runs locally on my own machine, I could optimize for control and privacy over scale: contacts and full send history live in a local SQLite file, and the AI key is held in memory only, never written to disk. There was no need for accounts, multi-tenancy, or a hosted backend. The whole product could focus on doing one person's outreach exceptionally well.

The funnel

FRAMING

I ran my own job search like a product. The outreach became a funnel with clear stages, contacts to sent to replied, and the whole app exists to move people through that funnel deliberately rather than by hand.

How it works

Import

Recruiter contacts come in by file, paste, or manual entry, with deduplication so the same person never lands in the pipeline twice.

Auto-enrich

Each contact is enriched automatically, guessing name, company, and recruiter type from the email, and flagging anything uncertain for a human look.

Review and approve

I confirm the guessed fields, set track and priority, and approve. Nothing sends without passing through this gate, which keeps quality high.

Send, paced

Approved emails go out from Gmail personalized per contact, paced across 8am to 4pm under a daily cap and a warm-up ramp that grows volume gradually.

Track replies and bounces

The app polls Gmail for replies and bounces and updates each contact's status, so the funnel always reflects reality.

Follow up automatically

Day-5 and Day-10 follow-ups are scheduled on business days, skipping weekends and US holidays. A reply cancels that contact's remaining follow-ups.

Key decisions

Two decisions defined the product. First, the warm-up ramp and daily cap: blasting cold email from a fresh sender is the fastest way into a spam folder, so I paced sending across the workday and grew daily volume gradually to protect deliverability. That is a deliverability decision dressed as a scheduling feature. Second, the human-in-the-loop review gate: auto-enrichment is fast but fallible, so rather than fully automate, I kept an explicit approval step. The app does the tedious 90%, and I make the judgment calls that actually affect whether an email lands well. Personalization stays grounded too: tailoring never invents facts about a recruiter or company.

Tech stack

FastAPI and Jinja2 for the app, SQLModel over SQLite for local data, APScheduler for the paced, scheduled sending, and the Gmail API over OAuth for delivery and reply tracking. Optional OpenAI polish does light tailoring on high-priority emails when I want it, and is off by default so most mail sends as clean, merged templates. The stack stayed intentionally small: everything runs on one machine with one local database.

The PM angle

Building this was as much a product exercise as an engineering one. I treated my search as a funnel and designed the system around its stages: import and enrichment widen the top, the review gate protects quality, paced sending and the warm-up ramp protect the channel itself, and follow-ups act as a retention loop that re-engages contacts who went quiet. Reply and bounce tracking is the feedback signal, the closest thing to product analytics, telling me which segments and templates are converting and which are not.

What I would measure

The metrics that matter mirror any conversion funnel: reply rate as the headline, bounce rate as a health check on list quality and deliverability, and follow-up lift, how many replies come from the Day-5 and Day-10 bumps rather than the first touch. Over time I would compare reply rates across recruiter segments and templates to learn what actually resonates, then feed that back into the defaults. The takeaway for me was that framing a messy personal task as a product, with a funnel, a quality gate, and a feedback loop, turned guesswork into something I could observe and improve.
