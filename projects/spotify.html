Spotify's Podcast Pivot

In 2019 Daniel Ek declared Spotify an "audio company" and spent over $1B on podcast studios and exclusives to escape a structural margin trap: roughly 70% of revenue flows to the labels, leaving gross margin near 26% against Netflix's 43%. I assessed the pivot five years on and judged it a business-model reinvention, not just a product expansion.

My role
Product Manager
Context
Product-transformation analysis, platform economics
Lens
Five-year retrospective, business-model strategy
Verdict
Right constraint, mixed execution

The situation

With 550M+ users, Spotify built the largest music streaming business in the world but on rented land. Roughly 70 cents of every revenue dollar flows to rights holders, holding gross margin near 26% against Netflix's 43%, and Spotify has almost no pricing power over Universal, Sony, and Warner. When it nudged the premium price from $9.99 to $10.99 in 2022, that single move triggered 2.3% premium churn, a vivid demonstration of how little headroom the model leaves. In 2019 Daniel Ek reframed the company as an "audio company" and committed over $1B to Gimlet, Anchor, and Parcast plus exclusives with Joe Rogan and the Obamas. The deeper claim I tested was that this was not a feature expansion but a business-model reinvention aimed at the structural margin trap of music.

THE CORE PROBLEM

Scaling music makes the margin worse, not better. Every incremental stream sends most of its value straight to the labels, so growth deepens dependence on the exact suppliers Spotify cannot negotiate down. Owning audio was the only lever that touched the cost structure itself.

The strategic question

Can Spotify shift from a pass-through music utility into a higher-margin audio platform it actually owns, and does owning content change its leverage over the labels? I framed it through the product lifecycle: music is a mature core and podcasts a growth bet, and both have to run in parallel rather than one cannibalizing the other. The market data backed the logic of diversifying: music streaming is a roughly $33B market growing near 8% a year, while podcasts are a roughly $26B market growing near 22%, a far steeper curve to ride.

Analysis: Porter, JTBD, and a broken context

Through Porter's Five Forces, music runs High on nearly every axis: the labels own the catalog Spotify cannot live without, and only the threat of new entrants reads moderate. Owning podcast content sidesteps that supplier entirely and opens ad-supported monetization the labels never touched. But the deeper finding came from Jobs to be Done: music is "lean-back" mood listening while podcasts are "lean-forward" attention, two genuinely different jobs. Injecting podcasts into mood playlists broke contextual integrity, what I called precision without empathy, optimizing for catalog reach while ignoring the listener's actual intent.

Lean-back

Music is hired for mood, identity, and ambient company. The job is to disappear into the background and require nothing.

Lean-forward

Podcasts are hired for focus and learning. The job demands deliberate attention, which is the opposite posture from a mood playlist.

The mismatch

Forcing both into one surface confused the experience. By 2025, 31% of users had sampled podcasts but only 8% listened regularly.

The monetization gap was just as telling: podcast ad revenue reached roughly $400M, only about 4 to 5% of total, with platform share split Spotify 31%, Apple 15%, and Amazon 13%. Meanwhile marketing spend ran about $1.2B, near 11% of revenue, to push a format most users were sampling but not keeping.

Reframing the North Star

I argued Spotify was optimizing the wrong thing. The right North Star Metric is Weekly Intentional Listening Hours per User (WILH), hours where the listener chose audio on purpose, guarded by music 90-day retention, ad complaint rate, and creator churn held at or below 5%. To prioritize the roadmap I combined MoSCoW with RICE: an Open Ad Marketplace scored 10.0 and a mode-aware UI 7.9, the two clear top bets. I structured delivery as dual-track agile against a 12-month roadmap with 2025 to 2027 milestones and explicit exit criteria.

STRATEGIC EVOLUTION

Three phases, one direction. The pivot moved from Exclusive-First (2019 to 2021), to Integrated (2021 to 2023), to Creator-First (2024 to 2025), a drift away from buying content toward enabling it that the recommendations should have arrived at sooner.

Tradeoffs

Over $1B of upfront exclusivity spend pressured profitability while underdelivering on the listening hours it was meant to move, and exclusivity actively fights discoverability. The clearest evidence: non-exclusive shows drove about 2.3x the listening hours of locked-up ones, so betting on exclusivity over creator tooling worked directly against the margin goal. The Rogan deal in particular bought attention but also brand-damaging controversy. Spotify also carries no ecosystem fallback: Apple subsidizes audio through hardware and Amazon through Prime, while Spotify lives or dies on audio alone.

My recommendations

Intentional audio platform

Reposition around a mode-aware UX that respects whether the listener is leaning back or leaning forward, instead of blending the two.

Empower creators

Ship a Creator Studio 2.0 and drop exclusivity, since non-exclusive shows already earn 2.3x the listening hours.

Short-form Clips

Use clips-first discovery to surface shows while controlling CDN cost, turning podcasts into a growth loop rather than a buried tab.

WILH-driven OKRs

Run the org off Weekly Intentional Listening Hours, plus ethical ad models and expansion into India, Indonesia, Brazil, and Mexico.

My takeaway

The pivot attacked the right constraint, supplier power, but the early optimism was not well-founded, so my verdict five years on is mixed. Spotify over-indexed on costly exclusivity when the durable lever was creator tooling and an open ad and audiobook marketplace. I believe the strategy should be judged by two numbers that cut through the noise: gross-margin expansion, and the share of listening hours sitting outside label-controlled music. Until those move, owning audio remains a promising thesis that the execution has not yet earned.
