ING Agile Banking

In September 2015 ING moved from silos to squads, restructuring roughly 3,500 headquarters employees into tribes, squads, and chapters borrowed from Spotify's model, inside a heavily regulated bank. I analyzed whether an agile operating model can survive compliance, legacy systems, and scale.

My role
Product Manager
Context
Operating-model transformation, regulated industry
The move
From silos to squads, September 2015
Verdict
Necessary but not sufficient

The situation

Facing digital-native competition and rising customer expectations, ING made a bold structural move in September 2015: it reorganized roughly 3,500 headquarters staff away from traditional departments and into a Spotify-style model. The choice is notable precisely because that model was built for a software company, not a regulated bank, which is the tension running through the whole analysis. ING was betting that the operating model, not just the technology, was what separated it from the digital challengers.

The new building blocks

Squads

Small cross-functional teams owning an end-to-end customer outcome, with the autonomy to ship without waiting on adjacent departments.

Tribes

Collections of squads working on a shared domain, providing alignment and a coherent mission without recreating the old silos.

Chapters

Functional skill lines that cut across squads, preserving deep expertise and consistent standards so autonomy does not fragment quality.

The strategic question

Can an agile operating model built for a software company work inside a regulated, legacy-heavy bank, where the cost of a mistake is regulatory and reputational, not just a rolled-back release? That asymmetry, the price of failure, is what makes the import non-trivial, and it is the question I set out to answer rather than assume.

Analysis: structure follows strategy

The underlying bet is that customer experience is now the competitive battleground, so the org must be reshaped around customer journeys, not banking functions. Cross-functional squads collapse the hand-offs between product, IT, and operations that used to slow every change to a crawl, while chapters preserve deep expertise so consistency does not erode. I read this as a deliberate answer to Conway's Law: if your systems mirror your communication structure, then redesigning the communication structure is how you eventually redesign the systems.

Why banking is harder than streaming

A music app can ship and roll back a feature in an afternoon. A bank cannot, and pretending otherwise is where most agile imports fail.

THE STRUCTURAL DIFFERENCE

Compliance, risk, and legacy core systems resist independent deployment. Squad autonomy has to coexist with governance, audit trails, and shared platforms, which is exactly where naive copies of the Spotify model break: the labels get adopted but the underlying conditions never do.

Tradeoffs

The model trades managerial control and predictability for speed and ownership, and it demands a hard cultural shift from hierarchy to trust that no org chart can mandate. The failure mode is clear: copying the labels of squads and tribes without the underlying autonomy, funding model, and platform investment yields agile theater rather than agile outcomes. The relabeling is easy and visible; the prerequisites are slow and invisible, which is why so many transformations stall at the cosmetic layer.

My recommendation

I would treat the structure as necessary but not sufficient. It has to be paired with continuous delivery on modernized platforms, with risk and compliance embedded inside squads rather than bolted on as a downstream gate, and with genuine leadership behavior change rather than a new vocabulary. Agile in a bank succeeds only when autonomy is matched by engineering and governance maturity. Get that pairing wrong and you have a relabeled org chart; get it right and the structure finally does the work it promised.
