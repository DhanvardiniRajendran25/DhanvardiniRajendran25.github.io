Target Inventory Collapse

Target went from chasing stockouts to drowning in the wrong inventory in under a year, with Q2 2022 operating margin collapsing to 1.2%. My thesis: this was structurally inevitable, not a forecasting miss. A supply chain optimized for availability under long lead times, paired with incentives that rewarded over-ordering, could only fail this way.

My role
Product Manager
Lens
Operating model, incentives, Goodhart-style metrics
Context
Target, 2022 inventory collapse and markdown cycle
Method
Six-part structural analysis, from product promise to incentives

The situation

In 2022 Target was caught with a glut of bulky, discretionary goods like furniture and home decor just as consumer demand swung back toward services and essentials. The aggressive markdowns and order cancellations that followed crushed profitability: Q2 2022 operating margin fell to just 1.2% as the company worked through excess inventory. The popular story was a forecasting failure. I argue the failure was structural.

The thesis

Not a forecasting failure

The collapse was structurally inevitable. A supply chain optimized for availability under long lead times, paired with incentives that rewarded over-ordering, could only fail this way. The forecast was a symptom, not the cause.

Was this a one-off bad bet, or the predictable failure mode of how Target's operating model and incentives translated demand signals into purchase orders? My six-part analysis traced it from the product promise through the operating model, information speed, risk tradeoffs, and the performance system. The answer was clear: the collapse was baked into the structure long before any single forecast, and the headline 1.2% operating margin was the bill coming due.

The core question

A supply chain that can chase availability across thousands of discretionary SKUs is, by construction, a supply chain that commits early and commits big. So the question I held onto was not "who got the forecast wrong" but "what would this system do when demand reversed mid-pipeline." Once framed that way, the markdown cycle reads less like a mistake and more like the only thing the structure could have produced.

Analysis: the structure that made it inevitable

Three layers stacked on top of each other turned a normal demand shift into a profitability event.

Operating model

Long-lead global sourcing, forward purchasing, and pre-positioned inventory create operational irreversibility: you cannot un-order goods already on the water.

Information vs execution

Forecasting actually detected the shift toward services and essentials. Information speed outran execution speed, so the in-transit pipeline kept arriving against collapsing sell-through.

Performance system

In-stock and sales-growth metrics rewarded over-ordering: a Goodhart-style trap where optimizing the chosen metric quietly degrades the outcome it was meant to proxy.

The bias toward more inventory was rational for every individual buyer and disastrous in aggregate, which is the signature of a structural problem rather than an individual one.

The asymmetric risk

The structural tensions are availability versus flexibility, scale versus responsiveness, and depth versus financial risk. Underneath them sits an asymmetric risk: a stockout costs a single sale, but over-ordering in a long-lead discretionary category converts a demand softening into a markdown liability that compounds across the whole pipeline. Because the metrics only priced the stockout side of that asymmetry, the system leaned permanently toward over-commitment. Nobody was being reckless, they were being rewarded.

My recommendation

Fix the structure, not the buyers, through three sequenced and interdependent changes. First, inventory segmentation: essentials keep forward cover while discretionary goods move to shorter commitment cycles. Second, lead-time reduction via a hybrid global-plus-nearshore sourcing model, so commitments are smaller and later. Third, and most important, a performance-metric redesign that rewards adaptability and inventory productivity rather than availability alone.

Why the sequence matters

The three changes are interdependent, and the order is the whole point. Changing metrics first, without segmentation or shorter lead times, just punishes buyers for a system they cannot control: you would tell them to be adaptable while the operating model still forces large, early, irreversible commitments. Segmentation and lead-time reduction create the room to act differently, and only then does redefining the metric actually change behavior. A redesign that ignores the sequence would look like reform and function like blame.

My takeaway

The lesson I carry from Target is that incentives are part of the supply chain. You can buy better forecasting tools and still collapse, because the system will faithfully execute whatever the metrics reward. As a PM, when I see a failure that everyone calls a one-off bad bet, my instinct now is to ask what the performance system was quietly optimizing for, and whether the structure left anyone the option to do otherwise. The most durable fix is almost never a better forecast, it is a structure and a scorecard that can survive being wrong.
