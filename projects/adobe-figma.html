Competitive Strategy

Adobe × Figma

In September 2022 Adobe agreed to pay roughly $20B for Figma, then in December 2023 the deal collapsed under regulatory pressure and Adobe paid a breakup fee near $1B. I analyzed why a profitable incumbent would pay that much for a smaller rival, and what the collapse left exposed: that collaboration is the product experience, not a feature.

The situation

Figma reinvented design as a multiplayer, browser-native, collaborative workflow and won the next generation of product teams, exactly the segment Adobe XD was built to defend and failed to hold. In September 2022 Adobe responded with the largest acquisition offer in its history, roughly $20B, only to walk away in December 2023 under regulatory pressure and pay a breakup fee near $1B. The episode is a clean case study in what happens when an incumbent meets a shift it cannot out-feature.

WHAT THE COLLAPSE EXPOSED

Collaboration is the product experience, not a feature. Adobe was willing to pay a record premium not for Figma's revenue but to absorb the way Figma had redefined the job, which is the real lesson the failed deal left behind.

The strategic question

Was this build, buy, or neutralize? Adobe had already tried to build, with XD, and lost. So the genuine question was whether to buy the platform shift outright or watch it erode the Creative Cloud franchise from the collaborative layer up. Framing the decision this way is what separates a panic purchase from a defensible strategic move, and it is the frame I used to judge the price.

Analysis: the moat under attack

Adobe's moat is workflow lock-in across creative professionals, but Figma did not attack the tools head on. It attacked from a different vector entirely: collaboration as the product experience, not a feature, with network effects that compound every time another teammate opens the same shared file. I read the shift through three lenses.

Product life cycle

Desktop design tools were maturing while multiplayer, browser-native design was still on its growth curve, so the value was migrating to the newer model.

Jobs to be Done

The job shifted from creating polished artifacts alone to working together in real time, a different job that XD's feature set never truly served.

Kano

Collaboration moved from a delighter to a basic expectation, meaning Adobe could no longer win simply by matching features one at a time.

This is a classic disruption-from-below pattern that incumbents rarely out-feature, which is precisely why buying looked cheaper than fighting.

Why the price made sense to Adobe

Sizing it through TAM, SAM, and SOM, the prize was the entire collaborative-design market Figma was defining, not its current revenue. The premium was really about neutralizing a category-defining threat and acquiring the collaborative architecture XD never delivered. For an incumbent, removing the disruptor and absorbing its network effect can be worth far more than the target's standalone economics, which is how a number that looks irrational on a revenue multiple becomes rational on a strategic one.

Tradeoffs and the collapse

Regulators read the deal the same way the market did. The UK's CMA, alongside EU scrutiny, treated it as eliminating a direct competitor, and in December 2023 the acquisition was abandoned with a breakup fee near $1B. That outcome left Adobe in the worst of both worlds: it had paid a fortune to fail, and it now faced the original problem against a stronger, better-capitalized, now-independent Figma that had just been validated as a genuine threat.

My takeaway

When a rival is winning on a structural shift you cannot copy, acquisition is rational but increasingly blocked by antitrust, so it is no longer a reliable strategy to lean on. Post-collapse I would have Adobe compete on the shift directly. I prioritized that response with MoSCoW and RICE and anchored it to a North Star built on collaborative engagement, rebuilding collaboration as a first-class primitive rather than a bolt-on feature, while defending the professional workflows where Adobe's real lock-in still holds. The deeper lesson: when the category redefines itself, you either redefine with it or pay to make it disappear, and the second option is closing fast.
