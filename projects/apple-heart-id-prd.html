02 · Executive summary

Your heartbeat is the password.

Apple Heart ID is a continuous, hands-free authentication method built on the one biometric a user cannot forget, leave at home, or hold wrong: the unique signature of their heartbeat. The Apple Watch ECG sensor captures the cardiac pattern, the Apple Neural Engine turns it into a private heart signature on-device, and wrist detection gates the credential so that simply wearing the watch unlocks iPhone, iPad, and Mac.

The product bet is that a passive, always-on biometric can serve the users that passwords, fingerprint, and Face ID fail most, older adults and differently-abled users, while raising the security ceiling for everyone. This PRD specifies that bet end to end: the system behavior, the data and privacy model, target FAR / FRR thresholds, the evaluation rubric, and the rollout.

03 · Background & context

Where this fits in Apple's authentication story.

Apple's authentication has moved steadily toward less friction: Passcode (2007), Touch ID (2013), Face ID (2017). Each step removed a barrier but kept a moment of explicit action, a touch, a glance, a hold. Heart ID is the next step: it removes the action entirely. Authentication becomes a continuous state rather than a discrete event.

Two things make now the moment. First, more than 60% of iPhone owners already own an Apple Watch, so the credential-bearing hardware is already on the wrist. Second, the Watch already ships a clinical-grade ECG sensor and an on-device Neural Engine, the exact pipeline a heart-signature biometric needs. The capability exists; no one has shipped a consumer-grade, ecosystem-wide version of it.

04 · Problem statement & evidence

Today's logins are barriers, not doors.

Passwords get forgotten. Fingerprints fail when hands are wet or worn. Face ID struggles with masks, glasses, and poor lighting. For older adults and differently-abled users, these methods often feel like obstacles, and many depend on a caregiver just to set them up, which costs them independence, dignity, and privacy.

Evidence, from research and validation

Sized and validated inside Apple's ecosystem through surveys, interviews across three user segments, and personas. The pain is measured, not assumed.

Jobs-to-be-done

  • When my hands are unsteady, worn, or occupied, I want a method that does not depend on them, so I can stay independent.

06 · Goals, success metrics & non-goals

Authentication that disappears.

North Star metric · FAA

Frictionless Authenticated Access (FAA), the share of unlocks a user completes continuously and hands-free, without a password, a re-scan, or a caregiver. It captures the whole promise: secure access that simply happens while the watch is on the wrist.

Goals

  • User goal: let users authenticate continuously and hands-free, independently and with dignity.
  • Business goal: open an accessibility-first wedge into a feature every Apple user benefits from, deepening ecosystem lock-in and the value of owning an Apple Watch.
  • Model goal: verify identity from the ECG heart signature on-device, at a target false-accept and false-reject rate that beats or matches existing biometrics, with negligible battery cost.

Non-goals (v1)

  • Apple ID authentication in non-Apple systems.
  • Non-watch heart-monitoring hardware as a credential source.

Core use cases

  • Continuous unlock: while the watch is on the wrist and verified, the user's iPhone, iPad, and Mac unlock with no action.
  • Wrist-off lock: taking the watch off (or losing wrist contact) immediately invalidates the live credential and re-arms the lock.
  • One-time enrollment: a single calibration creates the signature, which auto-syncs to every chosen device.
  • Fallback: when Heart ID cannot verify, the user falls back to passcode, Touch ID, or Face ID with no dead end.

10 · AI / biometric capability & system behavior

What the matcher does, and what it must never do.

The core capability is continuous ECG-signature matching. The Watch ECG sensor captures the cardiac pattern (ECG and heart-rate variability), the Apple Neural Engine extracts an embedding, and a matcher compares the live embedding to the enrolled template.

Inputs

The live ECG waveform and HRV features captured at the wrist, gated by wrist detection, plus the enrolled signature template stored on-device.

Outputs

A match / no-match decision and a confidence score that drives the authenticated session state, never a raw waveform leaving the device.

Probabilistic core, deterministic gate

The matcher is probabilistic: it produces a similarity score thresholded against a target. The session gate is deterministic: wrist-off, revocation, or a score below threshold locks the session, no matter what.

What it must never do

Never make a medical or diagnostic claim from the ECG. Never transmit raw signal off-device. Never grant a session when wrist detection fails or the score is below threshold.

11 · Data requirements

The signal stays on the wrist.

  • ECG signal data: the Watch ECG waveform plus derived HRV features, the raw input to enrollment and verification.
  • Enrollment data: a short, guided ECG capture per user, converted into a private template; raw enrollment samples are not retained after the template is built.
  • Evaluation set: a held-out, diverse cohort spanning the target segments (older adults, differently-abled users, and the broader base) and a range of cardiac conditions, used to tune and validate FAR / FRR thresholds.
  • Privacy: the heart signature is biometric data and is treated as health data; processing is on-device, the template is stored in the Secure Enclave, and the signal never leaves the device.
  • On-device processing: capture, embedding, and matching all run locally on the Apple Neural Engine, so no waveform or template is sent to the cloud.

12 · Model & matching strategy

On-device matching, thresholded for trust.

The Neural Engine produces a compact embedding of the live ECG signature and compares it to the enrolled template. A match is declared when the similarity score clears a tuned threshold; that threshold is the central tradeoff lever between false accepts and false rejects.

Threshold policy

The operating threshold is set to hit the security target (a low FAR) first, then tuned so the FRR stays low enough that the experience still feels frictionless. Because Heart ID is continuous, a single below-threshold reading does not slam the session shut; it triggers re-verification, and only sustained mismatch or wrist-off locks the session.

13 · UX requirements

The experience has to be invisible.

  • Zero-action unlock: in the happy path the user takes no action; devices unlock as they are picked up.
  • Guided, accessible setup: on-screen guidance and assistive-tech compatibility through the entire calibration flow.
  • Honest state: a clear, glanceable signal for whether Heart ID is active, verifying, or fell back.
  • Graceful fallback: when verification cannot complete, the fallback prompt appears immediately, with no confusing dead end.
  • Control: simple paths to re-calibrate, choose devices, and revoke the signature.

14 · Evaluation framework

FAR / FRR targets, and how we judge them.

A biometric is only as good as its error rates. The two that matter are the false-accept rate (FAR), the chance the system accepts the wrong person, and the false-reject rate (FRR), the chance it rejects the right person. Security sets a ceiling on FAR; trust sets a ceiling on FRR.

This is a biometric model, not an LLM

Heart ID is a biometric matcher, so it is judged by FAR, FRR, equal-error-rate, and anti-spoofing, never by an LLM-as-judge rubric. There is no generated text to grade; there are only genuine and impostor decisions to count, broken out across cohorts, conditions, and time.

Enrollment & evaluation dataset

The held-out evaluation set is built for coverage, not convenience: it must span who the user is, what shape their heart is in, and what they are doing when the signal is read. Every cell below is sampled deliberately so error rates can be sliced by it later.

FAR / FRR trade-off & equal-error-rate

FAR and FRR move against each other: tightening the match threshold to reject more impostors also rejects more genuine users. The operating point is chosen on that curve, security first, then trust.

Low thresholdFAR rises, FRR falls: convenient but insecure
EER pointFAR equals FRR: the curve's crossover, used to compare versions
Operating pointSet left of EER: FAR pinned to the security ceiling first
High thresholdFAR falls, FRR rises: secure but rejects real users

Because Heart ID is continuous, the operating point can sit on the secure side of the curve: a single below-threshold reading re-verifies rather than locking, so a low FAR does not force a painful FRR.

Spoof & presentation-attack testing

  • Replay attack: play a recorded ECG waveform into the matcher and confirm it is rejected; the wrist gate plus liveness must defeat playback.
  • Synthetic signal: generate a plausible ECG from population statistics and confirm it does not match any enrolled template.
  • Lifted template: attempt to authenticate with a copied or extracted template off the Secure Enclave and confirm it is unusable off-device.
  • Coercion / wrist transfer: confirm the credential invalidates the instant the watch leaves the enrolled wrist.

Longitudinal drift testing

A heart signature is not frozen: it shifts with age, fitness, illness, medication, and exertion. Drift testing re-tests the same enrolled people over time and across states so the threshold is set to absorb natural change without re-enrollment, and so degradation is caught before it reaches the field.

Drift sourceTestPass condition

Segment-level evaluation & fairness

Aggregate FAR / FRR can look healthy while a single cohort is failing. Every error rate is therefore reported sliced by each cohort axis, and the headline fairness bar is the gap between the best and worst cohort, not the average.

Fairness across cohorts is a release gate

The product does not ship if any cohort, by age, skin tone, or cardiac condition, carries a materially worse FRR than the base. The max FRR delta across cohorts is a guardrail with a hard ceiling, not a dashboard nicety, and it is checked before every threshold or model change.

14c · Confidence, fallback & change management

What happens when the signal is poor, and how change is controlled.

ConcernHow Heart ID handles it
Confidence / uncertainty handlingThe matcher emits a confidence score, not just a yes/no. A poor or low-confidence reading triggers re-verification, not a lock; on sustained low confidence the user falls back to passcode, Touch ID, or Face ID. The system never locks the user out of their own device.
Human-in-the-loopNot applicable: authentication is real-time and on-device, so there is no human reviewer in the loop. The documented safe path is the deterministic fallback to existing Apple auth, which is always reachable.
Monitoring / observabilityEvery decision is tagged on-device with the model version and the threshold version. Privacy-preserving, aggregated telemetry tracks FAR-proxy, FRR-proxy, fallback rate, latency, and battery by cohort. No raw ECG is ever observed or transmitted.
Model & threshold change managementModel and threshold versions are tracked and changed only after the held-out rubric, the spoof suite, and the fairness gate pass. Changes roll out staged, are reversible, and trip alarms on any cohort regression.

15 · Safety, privacy & security

Biometric data is treated as health data.

On-device only

Capture, embedding, and matching run on the Apple Neural Engine; no raw ECG signal leaves the device.

Secure Enclave

The heart signature is stored in the Secure Enclave, isolated from the OS and apps, like other Apple biometric templates.

Health-data compliance

Heart data is handled under health-data consent and privacy rules, with explicit, scoped, revocable permission.

Liveness & wrist gate

Continuous wrist detection acts as a liveness and presence check; the credential is invalid the moment the watch comes off.

16 · Analytics & experimentation

Measuring FAA without touching the signal.

  • Privacy-preserving telemetry: measure FAA, fallback usage, failure rate, and setup drop-off from on-device, aggregated, de-identified signals, never from raw ECG.
  • Threshold experiments: evaluate FAR / FRR threshold settings on the held-out cohort before any field change.
  • Pilot cohorts: run the accessibility pilot as a measured cohort with explicit FAA and guardrail targets.
  • Guardrail alarms: trip on rising fallback usage or failure rate, the earliest signs Heart ID is failing for someone.

17 · Non-functional requirements

Fast, light, always there.

  • Latency: verification must complete fast enough to feel instant on device pickup; no perceptible wait in the happy path.
  • Battery: continuous wrist-gated verification must add only negligible daily drain to the Watch.
  • Availability: the credential must be available whenever the watch is worn and verified; fallback must always be reachable when it is not.
  • Reliability: stable successful-auth rate across lighting, motion, and environmental conditions that defeat camera- and touch-based methods.

19 · Dependencies, risks & assumptions

What this depends on, and what could go wrong.

Dependencies: the Apple Watch ECG sensor, the Apple Neural Engine, wrist detection, the Secure Enclave, and cross-device sync. Assumptions: the iPhone-to-Watch ownership overlap holds (60%+), and an ECG heart signature is stable and distinctive enough for consumer-grade authentication.

20 · Rollout & monitoring

Accessibility pilot first, then the ecosystem.

  • Phase 1, accessibility pilot: run with older-adult and differently-abled users to pressure-test FAA and the failure-rate guardrail and to validate FAR / FRR in the field.
  • Phase 2, broader beta: expand to early adopters, validate battery impact and reliability across conditions.
  • Phase 3, ecosystem GA: general availability across iPhone, iPad, Mac, and Watch.
  • Monitoring: FAA as the headline, with fallback usage, failure rate, battery impact, and setup drop-off as live guardrails; alarms trip on any segment regressing.
